\begin{abstract}\label{sec:abstract}
 
In 2017, Google Research published \textit{Attention Is All You Need} \cite{vaswani2017attentionneed}.  Their paper introduced the Transformer architecture that reshaped modern AI.   Just five years later, OpenAI's ChatGPT \cite{chatgpt} triggered AI's mainstream adoption.

Yet while our screens flicker with progress, space travel and clean energy breakthroughs remain comparatively rare.

\textbf{Dude, Where's My Spaceship?}

If attention were all we needed to commercialize AI, this paper argues that precision aim is all we need to solve grand challenges in the physical world.

Specifically, we propose spacecraft propulsion via momentum transfer from targeted orbital impacts.  Consider a ``PuffSat,'' a single-use microsatellite with a mass of roughly 25–100 kilograms, comparable to the unofficial mass of a 27U CubeSat \cite{cubesat_advanced_standard}.  When triggered, a PuffSat rapidly converts nearly all its mass into a low-density gas, generated explosively or by atomizing cold solids or liquids (see \autoref{sec:puffsat_design}). Ultra-lightweight microthrusters and electronics let it make small navigation adjustments.  Deployed in sequence, these PuffSats gasify and strike a shock-absorbing pusher plate on a target rocket, each impact delivering a pulse of momentum.

Recent missions such as Proba-3 \cite{llorente2013proba3,zhukov2025proba3aspiics} demonstrate that millimeter-scale formation flying works, at least in high-altitude orbits. Therefore, most PuffSat navigation and control tasks can be handled with robust model predictive control (MPC) \cite{visors_formation_flying} and Kalman filtering (see \autoref{sec:neural_navigation}).  These classical techniques are data-efficient and interpretable, with formal performance and safety guarantees. For the more demanding conditions near low periapsis, classical control methods can be augmented with neural networks that warm-start the controller and learn from residual errors.  They also add resilience against lost PuffSats and other anomalies (see \autoref{sec:neural_navigation}).

This speculative white paper is a brainstorming exploration that could be followed by more rigorous analysis. If the ideas prove too advanced for near-term implementation, they can instead be fodder for hard science fiction. The paper summarizes and updates some of the writings from my own blog, ``Aim Is All You Need.''\cite{aim2024}

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Conceived in the 1950s and recently brought to a new audience by \textit{The Three-Body Problem} \cite{liu2014three}, Project Orion remains one of the most compelling rockets ever proposed. It is unique in combining specific impulses comparable to those of electric propulsion with thrust levels on par with chemical rockets \cite{projorion}.   Orion would propel a spacecraft by directing hypervelocity plasma from nuclear explosions onto a pusher plate. Despite its theoretical promise, Orion faced insurmountable challenges related to political feasibility, radioactive fallout, and the impractical mass requirements stemming from the high minimum yield of nuclear explosions \cite{balcomb1970nuclear}. Even so, Orion validated the physics of hypervelocity pulsed propulsion.

Our approach replaces nuclear bombs with precisely aimed, hypervelocity gas puffs from specialized ``PuffSat'' microsatellites (see \autoref{sec:puffsat_design}) sequentially impacting a rocket's pusher plate.  These gas puffs can be scaled down to practical sizes.  Unlike nuclear explosions, small hypervelocity gas impacts could be efficiently contained within a pulsed propulsion chamber. With gravity assists and the Oberth effect \cite{oberth_effect}, hypervelocity PuffSat impacts could achieve energy densities and specific impulses well above those of chemical rockets.

Picture two rockets. The first rocket deploys a series of these fast-moving PuffSats. As shown in \autoref{fig:PuffSat_impact}, the deployed PuffSats follow precise paths to a shock-absorbing pusher plate (see \autoref{sec:lightweight_pusher_plates}) on a separate target rocket. Each one gasifies and strikes the plate in sequence. These collisions deliver concentrated pulses of energy and momentum to the target vehicle.

\section{To The Moon by Smart Gas Balloon:  The Business Case For PuffSat Pulsed Propulsion}\label{sec:business_case}

\subsection{Making Starship's Excess Capacity Useful To Satellite Customers} \label{sec:starship_safelaunch}

SpaceX's Starship may be the first fully reusable rocket. At scale, this could reduce space flight costs.   However, the rocket's payload capacity probably exceeds today's space industry needs. For example, \textit{The New York Times} reported:

\begin{quote}
Carissa Christensen, the chief executive of Bryce Tech, an analytics firm that tracks the launch market, says launching Starship frequently will be key to closing SpaceX's business case, but finding customers to fill the rocket's giant payload capacity will be challenging. 
``Starship's payload capacity is huge; it's very, very big, and there aren't that many commercial uses today for a rocket that big,'' she said.  ``Maybe it'll be so cheap that it makes sense to launch satellites on it if it's not full or near full.''  \cite{nyt_starship_size}
\end{quote}

Although many satellites might be too small to fill up Starship, they can still be very expensive to build and test. For an extreme example, the James Webb Space Telescope \cite{james_webb_space_telescope} cost \$9.7 billion \cite{jwst_cost} to build. Losing a payload that costly to a launch failure would be catastrophic, so reliability matters as much as raw capacity. McKinsey \& Company argues:

\begin{quote}
Safety and reliability will continue to be overarching concerns, suggesting excellent execution will be table stakes for a competitive launch company. \cite{mckinsey_reliability}
\end{quote}

Once rockets start ferrying astronauts, reliability becomes non-negotiable. Fully stacked, Starship weighs \SI{5}{\kilo\tonne} at launch \cite{starship}, yet its empty mass is only \SI{300}{\tonne}. That's an awfully thin soda can strapped to a \SI{5}{\kilo\tonne} bomb that we're entrusting with billion-dollar satellites or human passengers.

By contrast, a suborbital rocket that merely reaches \SI{200}{\kilo\meter} in altitude might have a propellant mass fraction under 60 percent \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025}. This greater structural margin allows for sturdier, more reliable engines and extensive payload protection systems. Of course, such a suborbital rocket cannot reach orbit on its own.  It needs Starship to ``push'' it with PuffSat pulsed propulsion.

Starship launches PuffSats for pulsed propulsion into a highly eccentric orbit around Earth, with a deliberately low \SI{50}{\kilo\meter} perigee that deorbits spent hardware, and a high apogee (see \autoref{sec:handling_space_debris}). These PuffSats move near Earth's escape velocity at their roughly \SI{200}{\kilo\meter} interception altitude. The suborbital rocket rides the PuffSats'  momentum pulses to low Earth orbit. The PuffSat mass this takes is modest, comparable to the target rocket's own mass (see \autoref{tab:mass_scenarios}). While this approach reduces payload capacity compared to a single rocket system, most satellites are unable to take advantage of Starship's enormous payload volume anyway.

\subsection{The 200 Mile High Club} \label{sec:200_mile_high}
SpaceX proposes direct Earth-to-Earth travel \cite{earth_to_earth}, ballistically launching passengers directly between cities with Starship from offshore platforms. This idea faces several challenges:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Marine Environmental Impact:} Noise pollution from sea launches could disturb marine mammals. Launch noise and sonic booms at coastal ranges already trigger federal marine-mammal harassment findings for nearby seals and sea lions \cite{nmfs_vandenberg_2024}.
\item \textbf{Noise Over Populated Areas:} Starship's descent would generate loud sonic booms over cities.
\item \textbf{Logistical Inefficiency:} Offshore launch platforms, by necessity located far from land, would add hours to the journey for boarding and departure, undermining the benefit of rapid travel.
\item \textbf{Salt Water Corrosion:}  Salt water is highly corrosive.   This poses a major engineering problem for  offshore wind farms \cite{wind_turbine_corrosion}.   Starship would suffer similar degradation on offshore platforms.
\item \textbf{Strong Offshore Winds:} Coastal regions such as the North Sea off the UK experience sustained high wind conditions. These offshore winds induce structural fatigue in wind turbines \cite{north_sea_wind_fatigue}. Starship's $\approx \SI{120}{\meter}$ profile \cite{starship} makes it susceptible to wind loading. While orbital launches can flexibly await favorable weather, point-to-point passenger missions operate under strict schedules. This could force launches during wind conditions that exceed safety margins for crewed operations.

\end{itemize}



A suborbital rocket plane is a more practical alternative. To reach orbit, the suborbital rocket requires propulsion PuffSats launched by Starship.   Starship can be launched from remote locations where noise pollution is acceptable.  Our suborbital rocket plane could take off from conventional urban airports using standard aircraft engines, then switch to rocket propulsion at high altitudes over remote areas to ``skip'' above the atmosphere and intercept Starship's propulsion PuffSats.   While normal airports can handle kerosene or methane fuel, most lack the infrastructure for cryogenic liquid oxygen.

However, two solutions address the liquid oxygen challenge:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Air-breathing Scramjets (Optimistic Scenario):} If economical passenger scramjets become feasible, rockets could accelerate to around Mach 7 \cite{nasa_x43a} before a steep climb, eliminating the need for liquid oxygen. This would also reduce propellant mass. Unfortunately, the extreme aerodynamic stress on a scramjet airframe, and its cost, keep plain suborbital rockets the better near-term bet.
\item \textbf{Mid-air Oxygen Refueling:} Suborbital rocket planes could receive liquid oxygen from specialized aircraft launched from airports equipped with liquid oxygen storage. This avoids retrofitting every urban airport, requiring only a single airport near major destinations.   Mid-air refueling also reduces our rocket plane's takeoff weight.  Oxygen refueling would require research and development to create rigid cryotolerant pumps and docking mechanisms, distinct from the flexible hoses used in military operations today.
\end{itemize}

A formation of propulsion PuffSats launched in an eccentric Keplerian orbit pushes the rocket plane onto a ballistic arc. That arc lies in a plane through the Earth's center, so its ground path is a great circle. This does not restrict which cities are reachable. Any two points on the globe share a great circle, and the shorter arc of that circle is the geodesic between them \cite{docarmo_diffgeo, bowditch_navigator}. The formation's orbital plane can therefore be chosen to pass over both the departure airport and the destination.

Earth's rotation and oblateness do not change this. The orbital plane is aimed at the destination's position at arrival time. The $J_2$ oblateness perturbation over a single suborbital arc is deterministic. Both effects go into the launch targeting instead of an in-flight correction \cite{vallado_astrodynamics}. Connecting departure and arrival at a fixed flight time is a two-point boundary value problem with a Keplerian solution \cite{battin_astrodynamics}. A single formation designed for the trip can therefore place the rocket plane on target without a dedicated plane-change burn. This removes an onboard maneuvering stage and its propellant mass. A small terminal trim may still be carried to absorb navigation error, supplied either by onboard motors or a second PuffSat formation.

To avoid a sonic boom upon descent, the plane must decelerate before atmospheric reentry. This deceleration could be provided by another set of propulsion PuffSats traveling in a retrograde orbit relative to the plane. These reentry PuffSats could be in a circular low Earth orbit rather than a high eccentricity orbit, reducing the required mass.

\subsection{What to Expect When You're Expecting Bodily G-Force}\label{sec:pregnant_women}\cite{expecting_pregnancy_book}
Pregnant women are prohibited from piloting fighter jets due to both safety and ethical concerns \cite{usaf_pregnant_aircrew}. Elevated G-forces and the extreme physical stress from using an ejection seat pose serious risks to the pilot and the developing fetus \cite{pregnancy_gforce_risk}. Suborbital transit, like military aviation, subjects passengers to high, sustained accelerations.  A safe way to carry these vulnerable passengers would help suborbital transit become mainstream.  The obvious fix is to reduce peak acceleration, but there are limits to this strategy.  Because of gravity losses, lower accelerations increase the $\Delta V$ (delta-v) required for a given trip.  

One promising technique is to seat passengers so that forces act chest to back along the body's $Gx$ axis.   FAA guidance and pilot experience suggest this  force is well tolerated in healthy non-pregnant pilots \cite{lateral_acceleration}.  The FAA advisory also mentions that forces directed from the feet towards the head ($Gz-$) are the most dangerous for adults.   Babies are frequently oriented head down in the womb, so at Earth's 1 g they already endure a continuous force in the most dangerous direction, though fluid buoyancy ameliorates it. If a fetus tolerates the worst direction at 1 g, the tentative hypothesis is that it can tolerate 2 g in the $Gx$ direction adults find most benign.  Neck pillows and back supports can comfort infants and adult passengers.  While the rocket's pusher plate reduces vibrations, infants may also need seats designed to provide additional protection against shaking.

Unfortunately, I found no studies directly evaluating the safety of even moderate (2 g) $Gx$ acceleration for pregnant women or infants. We must validate safety protocols with multi-stage trials, beginning with mice and then non-human primates.  Because human infants' brains are larger and develop differently than those of non-human primates, only tests on human subjects can confirm that the acceleration mitigation procedures prevent harm.  If we can't establish that suborbital flight is safe for these groups, they must be excluded from suborbital flights.  

\section{How PuffSats Work}\label{sec:how_puffsats_work}

\subsection{PuffSat Design And Gas Generation Details}\label{sec:puffsat_design}
The optimal PuffSat design depends on the launch scenario and mission requirements.  Explosive PuffSats fit terrestrial launch, where their chemicals are easy to manufacture.  Ice and liquid-oxygen designs that follow use volatiles mined in space (see \autoref{sec:lunar_rockets_no_fuel} and \autoref{sec:lunar_mining}).
\subsubsection{Explosive PuffSats} \label{sec:explosive_puffsat}
Explosive PuffSats detonate at a precisely calibrated standoff distance from the pusher plate. This ensures rapid conversion of their mass into a high-velocity gas plume, shaped to distribute momentum evenly across the plate surface.  To minimize aerodynamic drag during flight, each PuffSat adopts an elongated cylindrical geometry aligned with the direction of travel (see \autoref{sec:formation_challenges_current_missions}). This shape also extends the duration of gas-plume interaction, reducing peak stress on the pusher plate without compromising momentum transfer efficiency. A small shaped section of the PuffSat's explosive charge can also eject non-volatile solid components away from the rocket before impact, as described in \autoref{item:discard_before_impact}.  A failure to detonate at the intended moment could damage the pusher plate or the rocket, so redundant initiation systems are essential.

As shown in \autoref{fig:explosive_puffsat}, each PuffSat carries its own detonation system. As a backup to ensure reliable triggering, the rocket emits a sparse layer of volatile material (e.g., water ice) a few meters ahead of the pusher plate just before each PuffSat arrives. Passing through this ice layer produces a shock that reliably triggers detonation; the flight explosive is chosen with a shock-initiation threshold the ice-layer impact comfortably exceeds. The geometry precludes a  PuffSat from reaching the rocket without first crashing through its ice layer. To minimize mass, the layers can be prefabricated as a sparse lattice or parallel wires. For instance, parallel ice wires with square \SI{500}{\micro\meter} cross sections  spaced \SI{5}{\centi\meter} apart have an areal density of only $\SI{20}{wires\per\meter} \times (\SI{500}{\micro\meter})^2 \times \SI{917}{\kilo\gram\per\cubic\meter} \approx \SI{5}{\gram\per\square\meter}$.  To improve handling and tensile strength during deployment, the ice wires can be coated with a micron-thin layer of metal or plastic. The coating needs to be light enough for orbital drag to deorbit it quickly, and thin enough that it doesn't pose a major collision hazard to other satellites. The coatings must also avoid toxic elements like fluorine, because they'll be vaporized upon impact.  

If frozen materials prove impractical, the wires could instead be constructed from low-mass plastic explosives, designed to detonate on PuffSat impact.  If the rocket misses a PuffSat interception after reaching  minimum orbital speed, the explosive wires would unfortunately become space debris. This debris could  persist for hours or days until eroded by atomic oxygen or slowed enough by atmospheric drag to re-enter. Another alternative is to replace ice wires with liquid sprays that induce a shock detonation of the PuffSat's explosives upon impact.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{images/Explosive PuffSat.png}
    \caption{Cross sectional diagram of the key parts of an explosive PuffSat}
    \label{fig:explosive_puffsat}
\end{figure}

This paper does not attempt to identify optimal explosive chemistries. That selection entails complex trade-offs in sensitivity, handling safety, cost, and environmental impact. These issues are better addressed in applied engineering work than in conceptual mission design. For modeling purposes in \autoref{sec:estimate_cold_gas}, we adopt ANFO \cite{fabin2021anfo} as a shock-insensitive, deliberately unsuitable proxy. Its low density increases drag for a given PuffSat mass, helping ensure our performance estimates remain conservative while avoiding dual-use implications.

To prevent outgassing, the PuffSat's explosives are encased in very thin layers of high performance polymers or metals. These liners can also reflect sunlight, further reducing temperatures to minimize the escape of volatile materials. The skin could contain a thin foam that prevents shock detonation from micrometeorites smaller than \SI{5}{\micro\meter}.  However, the skin should not be thick enough to protect against shock detonation from the ice wires we use as a detonator backup.  Larger micrometeorite impacts simply result in PuffSat losses.

\subsubsection{Solid Ice Based PuffSats} \label{sec:icy_puffsat}
PuffSats filled with lunar ice (see \autoref{sec:lunar_rockets_no_fuel}) do not primarily rely on chemical reactions to produce gas. Their high tensile skins absorb sunlight, melting the ice to water under mild pressure. Small explosives or an ice wire impactor (see \autoref{sec:explosive_puffsat}) rupture the skin to release the water. However, this release is too slow for complete vaporization before impact. Even if the water fully boiled, the steam expanding into vacuum would cool so rapidly that ice crystals would form.

Instead, the water is atomized into fine droplets, a process requiring much less energy than vaporization. To drive atomization, microscopic explosive particles are suspended in the water, either as a colloidal slurry or encapsulated in microbubbles.  The resulting mixture resembles a low-density version of the explosive emulsions used in terrestrial mining and construction \cite{emulsion_tutorial}.  The PuffSat's emulsion is diluted and tuned for spaceflight safety.

Unlike industrial emulsions designed for high-yield detonation, our explosive concentration is just sufficient to atomize the water. This keeps ISRU water mass high and risk low. Sensitizing agents are kept in a separate bladder inside the PuffSat. Mixing them into the main explosive liquid mass late in flight prevents premature activation, keeping the system stable during launch and transit.  

In microgravity there is no sedimentation to unmix the suspension, so the explosive particles stay uniformly distributed. These micro-explosives are chosen for precise shock sensitivity, ensuring they detonate upon high velocity impact with the incoming ice layer or in response to the main rupture charges. While likely imported from Earth due to manufacturing complexity, the micro-explosives' total mass is small compared to the bulk water payload.

Additional ice particles injected by the rocket ahead of the plume induce energetic turbulent mixing. The resulting aerodynamic shear, combined with micro-explosive detonations, fully atomizes the water into fine droplets before it reaches the rocket's pusher plate.

Where carbon is abundant, the meltwater can be lightly carbonated before flight. On rupture the dissolved carbon dioxide comes out of solution, and the expanding bubbles help shatter the water into fine droplets, the mechanism of effervescent atomization \cite{sovani2001effervescent}, an effect the vacuum pressure ratio makes strong even at a soda-like gas load.
We expect this to trim the micro-explosive load rather than replace it, and it is free only on the carbon-rich icy bodies, such as Ceres at roughly 20\% carbon \cite{marchi2019_ceres_carbon} or Phoebe with its trapped carbon dioxide ice \cite{phoebe}, not the carbon-poor Moon.

\subsubsection{Liquid Oxygen PuffSats}\label{sec:lox_puffsat}
PuffSats filled with lunar oxygen (see \autoref{sec:lunar_mining}) must also atomize before reaching the pusher plate. To retain liquid oxygen (LOX) during orbital transit, these PuffSats require thicker skins engineered for elevated internal pressures, cryotolerance, and thermal insulation. To minimize boiling of the highly pressurized LOX, PuffSats use high-reflectivity coatings such as emerging ``solar white'' yttrium oxide composites \cite{nasa_solar_white_y2o3} (see \autoref{sec:epstein_drives}).  These coatings are backed by emissive layers that radiate residual heat into space, maintaining cryogenic temperatures without active cooling.

A detachable standoff sunshade is a second passive-cooling option, beside the conformal solar-white coating above. The James Webb Space Telescope flies one: five layers of aluminized Kapton, the two sun-facing layers also coated in doped silicon, cooling its cold side to about \SI{40}{\kelvin} with no active refrigeration. It holds that temperature at the Sun-Earth L2 point, just beyond Earth's night side at roughly \SI{1.01}{\astronomicalunit} from the Sun \cite{jwst_sunshield, gardner2023jwst_mission}. That is flight proof that a passive shade reaches deep cryogenic temperatures at Earth's distance. For liquid oxygen the \SI{40}{\kelvin} reach is more headroom than needed, since oxygen freezes near \SI{54.4}{\kelvin} and stays liquid only up to about \SI{90}{\kelvin} \cite{nist_webbook_oxygen}. A LOX shade can therefore run lighter, with fewer layers tuned to hold roughly \SIrange{60}{80}{\kelvin}. The full \SI{40}{\kelvin} reach is the margin that keeps liquid hydrogen cold much farther from the Sun, out toward Ceres (see \autoref{sec:ceresly_good}).

The shade is jettisoned shortly before the PuffSat is atomized. A floppy multilayer cannot survive the collision, and it would scatter the gas plume. We do not keep it as reaction mass. A first-order estimate puts a \SIrange{1}{3}{\square\meter} shade at only \SIrange{0.2}{0.6}{\kilo\gram}, a few percent of a \SI{25}{\kilo\gram} PuffSat. Its aluminum and silicon metallization would also contaminate the non-combustible LOX pusher plate. That risk is worst at the cool \SI{3.2}{\kilo\meter\per\second} lunar-return end, where the thin film may not fully vaporize on contact. The release timing is set by plume geometry, not by boiloff. Unshaded at \SI{1}{\astronomicalunit}, the reflective PuffSat absorbs only \SIrange{10}{15}{\watt} and takes hours to warm by \SI{20}{\kelvin}, so a coast of seconds to minutes barely touches the LOX. The discarded Kapton has a high area-to-mass ratio and deorbits quickly, or it leaves on the dry-mass disposal path (see \autoref{item:discard_before_impact}).

Upon rupture, the thicker skin risks becoming hazardous shrapnel. While PuffSat skins are engineered to retain cryogenic LOX under elevated internal pressure, they need not be shock-resistant (though an outer protection against micron-scale micrometeorites is still required). Selecting materials prone to brittle fracture at low temperatures may enhance debris mitigation by ensuring the skin fragments on impact. The rocket releases a denser intercepting ice layer that shatters the brittle skin in a process analogous to how Whipple shielding breaks up space debris into less threatening dust \cite{whipple_shield}.

As with the icy PuffSats discussed in \autoref{sec:icy_puffsat}, the liquid oxygen is atomized through a combination of micro-explosive detonation, aerodynamic shear, and shock interaction with the ice layer.  The pusher plate for liquid oxygen PuffSats must be lined with a non-combustible material.

\subsubsection{Balloon PuffSats}
Inflated balloon PuffSats avoid the catastrophic risk of failed detonations.  When filled with gaseous water vapor or oxygen, balloon PuffSats can operate without advanced reflective cooling. Project Echo \cite{nasa_echo_ii_1966} demonstrated that large orbital balloons can be built from low-cost materials such as metalized mylar.

At low periapsis, maintaining balloon navigation control and structural integrity is difficult because of  atmospheric drag, debris impacts, and radiation pressure. Large balloon surface area also demands more polymer, increasing costs. If high performance polymer costs drop, balloon PuffSats could become a favorable option.

\subsection{Comparison To Pellet Beam Propulsion Prior Art}
In 2001, Gerald Nordley proposed an externally pulsed propulsion system in which hypervelocity pellets, capable of self-steering, transfer momentum to a spacecraft via impact with a pusher plate \cite{nordley2001interstellar}. This concept built upon earlier work by Clifford Singer \cite{singer1979interstellar}, who envisioned unguided macroscopic projectiles launched at extreme velocities. Nordley and Crowl survey this family of mass-beam propulsion concepts \cite{nordley2015massbeam}.

Both Singer's and Nordley's designs relied on mass drivers.  These devices would need to accelerate sizable objects to very high speeds, posing formidable engineering challenges. To address this, Jordan Kare introduced the SailBeam concept \cite{kare2001sailbeam}, which replaced the mass driver with solar or laser driven miniature sails. Kare further proposed vaporizing the sails upon impact and using a magnetic pusher plate to convert the resulting plasma into thrust.   Artur Davoyan received NASA funding to improve the efficiency of Kare's ideas by accelerating pellets using laser ablation rather than radiation pressure alone \cite{davoyan2023pelletbeam}.

These pellet stream propulsion ideas often prioritized interstellar speeds over economic feasibility.  Instead of using expensive mass drivers or light sails, our PuffSat pulsed propulsion system focuses on achieving high speeds primarily through low cost orbital maneuvers.  Unlike pellet beam proposals, PuffSat pulsed propulsion provides very heavy lift to propel large craft into orbit.  Those proposals also leave unexamined the near-term power sources needed to lift multi-ton objects into orbit. Our PuffSats can weigh kilograms, and are composed mostly of materials that gasify to biocompatible chemicals like water vapor, nitrogen, oxygen, and carbon dioxide.   This means they generate less pollution for a given $\Delta V$, which makes them practical for very large-scale near-Earth applications.  

Although our PuffSats contain electronics, the majority of their mass consists of inexpensive explosives or ices. This composition enables comparable propulsion at lower cost than systems dominated by electronics or solar sail material. Water ice, in particular, is abundant and mineable throughout the solar system.  Larger PuffSats can afford heavier electronics than Nordley's snowflake-sized guided pellets.  Beamed-plasma proposals like MagBeam push on a spacecraft's magnetic field with plasma streamed from a stationary platform \cite{winglee_magbeam}. While PuffSats could, in principle, interact with magnetic pusher plates, their largely gaseous composition (after gas generation) may make direct kinetic collision a simpler and more dependable mechanism for momentum transfer.  However, magnetic technologies would still be useful in the shock-absorber behind the pusher plate (see \autoref{sec:lightweight_pusher_plates}).  If a dense flow of small pellets hits a rocket, the resulting plumes may interfere with each other.   PuffSats have larger pulses spaced far enough apart that plume interference is negligible.

\subsection{Mass Fraction Of Rocket To PuffSat Mass}\label{sec:mass_fraction}
For PuffSat pulsed propulsion to be a viable option, the ratio of PuffSat gas mass to target rocket mass must be low for relevant rocket velocity changes. We develop a closed-form approximation for this ratio (\autoref{eq:PuffSat_ratio} in \autoref{sec:PuffSat_ratio_approximation}). Since real collisions are not perfectly elastic, the approximation includes an energy-loss ``fudge factor'' $f$. When all the gas strikes the plate, $f$ ranges from $0.5$ for a perfectly inelastic impact, where the gas sticks, up to $1$ for a perfectly elastic one, where it rebounds and pushes the plate about twice as hard. If some of the gas misses the plate, $f$ drops below $0.5$.

Project Orion's findings justify a high $f$. The Orion team determined that collisions at the pusher plate could be optically opaque \cite{balcomb1970nuclear}. This follows from Kramers' opacity law \cite{hansen_kawaler_trimble_stellar_interiors,kippenhahn_weigert_weiss_stellar,pols2011stellar}, in which opacity rises with density and falls with temperature. The plasma at the pusher plate is orders of magnitude denser than the low-density, inelastic plasma collisions studied in fields like fusion research, and the preliminary hydrodynamic analysis cited below finds that the density term prevails at impact conditions. An opaque plasma reabsorbs and reflects its own thermal radiation instead of letting it escape into the plate as heat. Little kinetic energy is lost to heating the plate, so the impact stays close to elastic.

Two design choices raise $f$ further. Spraying a thin layer of dark ablative material on the plate between impacts improves both its durability and its opacity (see \autoref{sec:lightweight_pusher_plates}). A curved, roughly parabolic plate reflects the gas back in a highly collimated stream. We select a high $f=0.8$, which appears reasonable from preliminary single-code hydrodynamic analysis \cite{Katz_puffsat_impact_sim_2026}, though the mission scenarios in this paper remain viable at more conservative values.

Relevant mass ratios and mission scenarios are summarized in \autoref{tab:mass_scenarios}, where several rows preview missions developed in later sections. The table shows PuffSat pulsed propulsion can lift tens of tonnes to orbit. For instance, if a reusable rocket like SpaceX Starship \cite{starship} lifts \SI{25}{\tonne} of PuffSats into a trans-lunar orbit, they can propel a  \SI{32}{\tonne} target craft into low Earth orbit. This exceeds both the Space Shuttle's maximum payload capacity \cite{nasa_wings_in_orbit} and the zero-fuel mass of a smaller regional jet like the Embraer E170 \cite{embraer_e170}.

\section{I Love ISRU.  Cheaper PuffSat Pulsed Propulsion \textit{Without} Giant Reusable Rockets}
For PuffSat pulsed propulsion of intercity passengers (see \autoref{sec:200_mile_high}) to achieve genuine economic competitiveness, the cost of launching propulsion PuffSats must be exceptionally low.  The rocket plane must be reliable enough to guarantee on-time takeoffs and avoid missing its initial PuffSat rendezvous.  Rocket acceleration and passenger cabin vibrations must be limited for infants and other susceptible passengers (see \autoref{sec:pregnant_women}), further increasing costs and $\Delta V$.  Even highly reusable rockets may prove too expensive, limiting this technology primarily to the high-end private aviation market. While luxury aviation customers typically prioritize flexibility, PuffSat propelled flights require advance scheduling.  Despite these limitations, the potential market for affluent travel between cities like Dubai and Dallas is likely at least an order of magnitude larger than the satellite launch market \cite{sia_satellite_industry_2025, iata_air_transport_2024}.

Still, it would be disappointing if we can't make suborbital travel mainstream.   If we can launch our PuffSats without giant rockets, we can reduce cost.   After all, ``the best part is no part,'' \cite{best_part_no_part} and a reusable rocket is one heck of a part.

One approach to launching these PuffSats into orbit involves using rockets powered by lunar-derived propellants. The space community is enthusiastic about water electrolysis to produce and store cryogenic fuel for lunar rockets \cite{nasa_water}. However, this process is energy-intensive and requires complex cryogenic infrastructure, particularly for hydrogen fuel. While these figures are an improvement over Earth-launched rockets, the high propellant mass fractions remain a serious obstacle.   

\subsection{Lunar Rockets Without Lunar Rocket Fuel} \label{sec:lunar_rockets_no_fuel}
Instead of launching our lunar volatiles towards Earth with conventional rocket fuel, we launch off the moon using the same PuffSat pulsed propulsion we've been discussing for terrestrial launches.   A rocket launched with PuffSat pulsed propulsion from the lunar surface into a trans-lunar injection orbit can burn its engines at periapsis around Earth.  Due to the Oberth effect, a small burn near Earth returns the rocket to the moon with a large velocity change.  As the rocket approaches the moon, it deploys its payload of PuffSats previously filled with lunar volatiles.  These returning PuffSats can push a new, more massive rocket filled with fresh volatiles off the moon.   We can then repeat this cycle for exponential growth in lunar PuffSat launch mass capacity.   We've reduced combustible fuel needs to one small periapsis burn, fueled by a charge carried from Earth, so no rocket fuel must be manufactured on the Moon.

However, we ideally don't want to make any chemical rocket fuel at all.   Let's again use PuffSat propulsion to launch our PuffSat deployment rocket from the lunar surface into a trans-lunar orbit.   We also launch a second set of PuffSats from the lunar surface into a retrograde trans-lunar orbit. As depicted in \autoref{tab:pulsed_combustion_illustration}, we trap the exploding gas from these collisions in a pulsed propulsion chamber and redirect this gas for thrust.   To use the Oberth effect and maximize gas collision velocity, we collide our PuffSats close to periapsis near Earth.

If our colliding PuffSats each travel at $v_p=\SI{11}{\kilo\meter\per\second}$, then from \autoref{eq:max_m_rp} derived in \autoref{sec:dv_effective},  the maximum theoretical combined effective exhaust velocity is $v_e = \frac{1}{2}v_p = \SI{5.5}{\kilo\meter\per\second}$.  Suppose this pulsed explosion has real-world losses for an effective exhaust of $v_e = \SI{3}{\kilo\meter\per\second}$.   If the main rocket accelerates at its Earth periapsis with a burn of \SI{1.9}{\kilo\meter\per\second} it will reach the moon at \SI{7.2}{\kilo\meter\per\second} \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025}.   If the rocket deploys propulsion PuffSats on lunar approach, they can push another rocket off the moon with a mass greater than the incoming PuffSats. Specifically, the incoming propulsion PuffSats have enough momentum to start an exponential growth cycle where each loop around the moon launches about 1.455 times the starting mass.   If we do this once per sidereal lunar month, we have increased our initial launch mass capacity by a factor of 1 million in about 2.8 years \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025}.

\subsection{Lunar Oxygen Has Mass And That Is All It Needs}\label{sec:lunar_mining}
Lunar volatiles like water ice are ideal for filling PuffSats, but they are scarce.   However, oxygen is the most abundant element in lunar regolith, roughly 45\% by mass \cite{mckay1991lunar}, locked up in its minerals.   Using solar energy and mining rovers derived from platforms like NASA's IPEx (ISRU Pilot Excavator) \cite{ipex_pilot_excavator}, we can produce oxygen.  On the volatile-depleted sunlit lunar surface, finding something to burn oxygen with for rocket propulsion is hard.   But PuffSat pulsed propulsion does not require oxygen combustion, just gas momentum.  In shadowed craters or artificial shade, lunar derived oxygen will liquefy and perhaps even solidify for storage.  

We use some of our lunar PuffSats for pulsed propulsion to lift terrestrial suborbital supply rockets into low Earth orbit.  The supplies include solar panels, pusher plates, PuffSat skins, electronics, and rovers. These supply rockets then use conventional engines for the trans-lunar leg so they can resupply our lunar surface operations.   When they reach the moon, we also use PuffSat pulsed propulsion to decelerate for lunar landing.  The deceleration propulsion PuffSats would previously have been launched from the moon's surface into an eccentric lunar orbit opposite to the incoming supplies' velocity.   We can optionally reduce terrestrial resupply requirements by embracing basic lunar manufacturing using metals like iron that are byproducts of lunar oxygen production.  These lunar metals could be shaped into pusher plate and pulsed propulsion chamber components.  

We use the rest of our lunar PuffSats to exponentially grow our PuffSat mass capacity in orbit using the Oberth cycle discussed in \autoref{sec:lunar_rockets_no_fuel}.  Oxygen production is very energy-intensive, and abrasive lunar regolith will likely degrade machinery faster than it would on Earth. Apollo surface suits were more worn after eight hours of lunar activity than training suits were after a hundred \cite{gaier2005lunardust}.  Regolith-resistant machines may still enable propulsion at costs that make hypersonic travel scalable.

\subsection{Jurassic Dark: Lunar Cold Trap Paleontology and PuffSat Mining Ethics}\label{sec:jurassic_dark}
In \autoref{sec:lunar_rockets_no_fuel}, we explored using lunar volatiles from permanently shadowed craters for PuffSat pulsed propulsion. These cold craters could also preserve biomolecules and organic biomarkers from Earth impact ejecta over very long timescales \cite{dino_dna}. While speculative, the scientific value of lunar cold trap paleontology obligates us to check for frozen fossil biomarkers as we mine lunar ice for PuffSat propulsion. The ethical tension is direct. The same excavation that fills PuffSats destroys the layered record it samples, so a survey must precede mining rather than run alongside it.

We could analyze the volatiles we're extracting to see if they contain homochiral organic molecules. Near-total single-handedness is a strong biosignature, since nonbiological chemistry tends to yield racemic mixtures (equal amounts of both handedness) or only small imbalances between them \cite{glavin2020chiral}.   Studying samples that contain homochiral molecules could reveal much of Earth's biological history, including how life began. And, of course, the discovery of dinosaur DNA would surely delight \textit{Jurassic Park} \cite{jurassic_park} fans.

\section{Sorry, I Don't Need ISRU}
\subsection{The Need For Speed} \label{sec:no_isru_rocket}\cite{topgun1986needforspeed}
If our propulsion PuffSats can reach high enough velocities, we could directly launch terrestrial payloads heavier than the PuffSats themselves.   This would eliminate the need for lunar ISRU. Repeatedly executing this launch cycle with ever larger (or more numerous) payloads would enable exponential growth in our launch capacity.

Consider a rocket launched from Earth carrying a pulsed propulsion payload.   We send $\frac{3}{4}$ of our payload on a prograde trajectory with a solar periapsis similar to the Parker Solar Probe and the remaining quarter on the opposing retrograde trajectory with the same Sun-grazing periapsis.  These trajectories require high $\Delta V$ but we can use Jupiter gravity assists to achieve them (see \autoref{sec:jupiter_gravity_initial}).   These payloads release micro-thrust, precision-steered projectiles (see \autoref{sec:solid_PuffSats}) which then collide sequentially at periapsis in a pulsed propulsion chamber as described in \autoref{sec:dv_effective}. In propulsion chambers that minimize energy losses, the resulting plasma can produce effective exhaust velocities of \SI{100}{\kilo\meter\per\second} or higher, depending on periapsis. Perhaps we accelerate from \SI{200}{\kilo\meter\per\second} to \SI{250}{\kilo\meter\per\second} with Earth crossing speeds around \SI{150}{\kilo\meter\per\second} \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025}.  At these Earth crossing velocities, incoming PuffSats could collide with PuffSats aboard a new terrestrial rocket in the rocket's propulsion chamber.  With the thrust from these collisions, our payload would then be sent on prograde and retrograde orbits with low solar periapsis, repeating the cycle.

If desired, we can optimize exhaust velocity further by lowering the periapsis in steps, so the payload falls farther toward the Sun between collisions.  This way, collisions after each periapsis reduction occur at progressively higher velocities for greater effective exhaust velocity.   If we double payload mass every 6 months, we increase our initial payload by a factor of 1 million in less than a decade \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025}. 

\subsection{Radiative Differences From Project Orion}\label{sec:radiative_differences}
Project Orion required shaped explosives to redirect plasma towards the pusher plate because the bomb's prompt energy was mostly emitted as X-rays that had to be converted into directed kinetic propellant \cite{balcomb1970nuclear}.  In contrast, our proposed system anticipates minimal radiative losses from collisions, even at speeds matching the Parker Solar Probe. Our reasoning for this low loss stems from
\begin{itemize}
    \item The colliding atoms in our system have a much lower atomic mass than fissile atoms. For a given total kinetic energy, the energy is distributed among a greater number of particles, giving lower average kinetic energy per particle and therefore lower plasma temperatures. Plasmas from lighter elements also have higher specific heat capacity, meaning they absorb more energy for a given temperature increase. 
    \item Fission fragments recoil at a few percent of the speed of light, roughly \SI{10000}{\kilo\meter\per\second} \cite{krane1988nuclear}. An inefficient bomb whose expanding plasma carries about 1\% of that kinetic energy moves near one-tenth the fragment speed, roughly \SI{1000}{\kilo\meter\per\second}, so our colliding atoms at a couple hundred \si{\kilo\meter\per\second} are about one-fifth as fast. A thermalized plasma's temperature scales as $m\langle v^2\rangle$, so this velocity ratio alone suggests peak temperatures at least 25 times lower.
    \item Folding the lower atomic mass into the same $m\langle v^2\rangle$ scaling puts temperatures perhaps 200 times lower in our collision than in a bomb. Radiative flux rises with the fourth power of temperature ($T^4$) \cite{incropera_heat_transfer}, so this points to a peak flux many orders of magnitude below that of the least efficient nuclear bombs, naively $200^4 \approx 10^9$. That figure is an order-of-magnitude plausibility bound, not a computed result.
    \item The colliding masses should be much smaller than a nuclear bomb, with a lower radiative surface area.   As the collision plasma expands adiabatically, a small plasma's volume grows proportionally faster than a large bomb plasma's for the same absolute expansion. The faster volume growth drops the temperature more quickly, cutting radiative output.
\end{itemize}

Lower temperatures generate longer-wavelength blackbody X-rays compared to those from bombs. This means materials with lower atomic numbers (lower Z) can absorb this radiation and keep the plasma opaque. While Project Orion favored expensive, difficult-to-work-with tungsten as the shaped-charge propellant slab \cite{balcomb1970nuclear, dyson2002project_orion}, these longer wavelengths should allow for the use of cheaper elements to achieve plasma opacity.

Opacity keeps the plasma pushing on a physical surface. At the highest near-Sun collision energies, a magnetic nozzle, a shaped magnetic field that redirects the ionized plasma with no wall in contact, may beat a solid absorber that has to survive each impact \cite{ahedo2010magnetic}.

\subsection{Solid Projectiles Rather Than Gas Generating PuffSats}\label{sec:solid_PuffSats}
Given the extreme velocities at periapsis, we switch from low-density PuffSat-generated gases to solid projectiles, which flash to opaque plasma on impact.   Despite the harsh conditions, the corona's low perturbation environment (see \autoref{sec:periapsis_challenges}) enables high precision.  Like Proba-3, we target millimeter-scale precision for head-on hits on small solids.   The same opacity argument that keeps Orion's pusher-plate plasma near-elastic (see \autoref{sec:mass_fraction}) applies here, so these plasma explosions should lose little energy to radiation \cite{balcomb1970nuclear}. The extreme temperatures and near-perfect targeting should equilibrate the plasma thermally before it expands.

We'd make these solid projectiles primarily from cheap low atomic number (low Z) materials like the carbon materials and ceramics that dominated the Parker Solar Probe's thermal protection system \cite{parker_thermal_protection}.   A small amount of cheap higher Z materials like iron would be included to ensure opacity to the blackbody X-rays produced.  
All the projectile's mass contributes to propulsion, irrespective of its composition.  As shown in \autoref{fig:parker_cross_section}, the projectiles can contain electronics, heat shielding, and some liquid that passively or actively cools the skin through a centrifugally driven convection cycle.    When close to the Sun, they can operate on batteries or compact heat engines, like MEMS-scale Stirling engines \cite{mems_stirling_engine}. These alternatives are good options where intense near-Sun sunlight can degrade traditional photovoltaics.

One possible low-cost projectile architecture might use graphite as both a structural backbone and thermal conductor, channeling heat toward shadowed radiators.  If the graphite can be cheaply made into insulating carbon foams, these foams will also reduce heat flux towards electronics.  The foams don't need to be optimized for low density like the Parker Solar Probe since all the projectile mass contributes to energy on impact. A bonding layer joins a thin ceramic skin designed to reflect incident solar flux. Ceramic or ablative heat shields protect electronics and propulsion systems housed in permanently shadowed zones. Given the low raw material costs of graphite and ceramics, large-scale manufacturing could drive per-kilogram projectile costs down to just a few dollars.

\subsection{Navigation Challenges Near Periapsis}\label{sec:periapsis_challenges}
Close solar periapsis poses several challenges. Sensors using visible light must filter solar illumination, and instruments must withstand heat and solar wind. Relativistic effects are predictable but must enter the trajectory calculations, and the high collision speeds demand extremely accurate navigation. The pulsed propulsion chamber may ablate slightly on each pulse, and the projectiles must be miniaturized to at most a few kilograms to contain the immense energy released at these velocities. The biggest problem is managing unpredictable changes in solar luminosity. Average radiation pressure can be baked into our navigation, but variations from sunspots and other magnetic phenomena complicate guidance prediction. Recent research suggests neural networks can improve the relevant weather predictions \cite{muralikrishna2022solar}.

Despite the extreme environment, solar approaches to within the outer corona experience relatively mild total perturbations. The outer corona is tenuous \cite{corona_density}, far below the residual-atmosphere densities that drag low- and very-low Earth orbit satellites \cite{leo_orbit_perturbation_simulation}. Combined with the predictable gravitational environment near solar periapsis, this leaves the unpredictable share of drag and gravitational variation smaller in the outer corona than in low-Earth orbit.
  
Spreading the push gently over minutes would require a large time gap between successive PuffSats. That gap is set by orbital phasing and stays fixed around the orbit. The PuffSats sit farthest apart at periapsis, where they move fastest, and bunch together at larger radii, where they move slower. These two effects cancel, so the arrival cadence at the rocket is the same everywhere. Because periapsis speeds are so high, even a large and hard-to-maintain spacing yields only a brief gap between arrivals. The Earth-crossing impacts therefore land in a short window and impose high acceleration, not the minutes-long push a crewed vehicle needs. The payloads we accelerate directly this way should be unmanned.   

When we lift high value payloads like astronauts into low Earth orbit, we use two sets of propulsion PuffSats. First, our Earth crossing PuffSats from the Sun push unmanned payloads into eccentric orbits around Earth. Then these eccentric payloads deploy PuffSats to push high-value cargo like people or  satellites just as we discussed in \autoref{sec:starship_safelaunch}.

\subsection{Initial Periapsis Orbits With Jupiter Gravity Assists} \label{sec:jupiter_gravity_initial}
A final issue is placing the initial payload in a retrograde low periapsis solar orbit.  The best approach for this is likely using a gravity assist from Jupiter, which was the original plan for the Parker Solar Probe mission \cite{mccomas2005solar}. 

To operate in deep space without relying on large solar arrays, the spacecraft may draw power from fuel cells or batteries. However, electrochemical systems are difficult to optimize under extreme cold. To mitigate thermal constraints, exothermic fuels can provide localized heating.  Alternatively, power can be generated via miniaturized chemically driven heat engines such as MEMS-scale gas turbines \cite{mems_gas_turbine}. If these energy sources use explosive reactants or yield volatile reactant products, these compounds can later be repurposed as PuffSat propellants. They then contribute to momentum transfer on impact. Near Jupiter, the PuffSats' volatiles would freeze and shield the spacecraft from radiation. The internal power source and shielding infrastructure would permit a close Jupiter flyby that maximizes our gravitational slingshot.

Optionally we can push more mass into the initial orbit by using a Jupiter gravity assist to place propulsion PuffSats into a retrograde Hohmann trajectory back to Earth. We can add a burn at Jupiter to use its Oberth effect.   They would cross Earth at around \SI{69}{\kilo\meter\per\second}, which could push a larger terrestrial payload onto  prograde and retrograde colliding trajectories at low solar periapsis.

\subsection{Jupiter-Only Exponential Launch Growth}\label{sec:jupiter_only_growth}
A simpler way to bootstrap an exponential launch cycle is to use the retrograde Hohmann trajectory from Jupiter described in \autoref{sec:jupiter_gravity_initial} but without close solar approaches.   PuffSats returning from Jupiter can push more than 9 times their weight into a highly eccentric orbit around Earth.   A small conventional burn coupled with solar electric engines and gravity assists with the inner planets can return this payload to Jupiter and restart the cycle.     The growth is slow compared to other proposals but may provide sufficient launch capacity to supply the satellite market within a few decades.

We can also combine this Jupiter launch cycle with reusable rockets or the lunar ice based cycle discussed in \autoref{sec:lunar_rockets_no_fuel}.  By increasing the number of initial PuffSat launches toward Jupiter, we can more rapidly scale up our launch throughput. Once throughput is up, Earth-based payloads can ride the returning PuffSats to orbit.

\subsubsection{Inner Planet Assist Alternatives}
PuffSats can employ gravity assists from the inner planets as alternatives to the Jupiter-only cycle. By using electric propulsion and deep-space maneuvers, these spacecraft can redirect their trajectories back to Earth at high velocities. Compared to Jupiter, inner-planet encounters occur more frequently and require less $\Delta V$ to reach from Earth.   

Flybys of the inner planets yield much lower Earth-relative velocities, typically between \SI{10}{\kilo\meter\per\second} and \SI{20}{\kilo\meter\per\second}.  This is less than the \SI{69}{\kilo\meter\per\second} return velocity from Jupiter. These lower velocities limit the payload multiplication achievable per cycle. However, the faster turnaround time of inner planet maneuvers can offset this limitation. Total payload growth rates end up comparable to those of Jovian reverse Hohmann transfers. 

Inner solar system trajectories can also use solar electric propulsion, which allows gradual
$\Delta V$ accumulation over long arcs in strong sunlight. These low-thrust arcs can be paired with high-thrust burns near perihelion or during flybys, which increase orbital eccentricity and aphelion, enhancing Earth-relative velocities for PuffSat pulsed propulsion.
 
Combining inner-planet and Jupiter assists raises throughput by giving more favorable launch windows.

\subsubsection{Inner Planets Combined With Lunar ISRU}
An object launched from the Moon towards Earth can reach Venus or Mars with modest additional maneuvers.  If its rockets fire at a \SI{200}{\kilo\meter} perigee, only \SIrange{300}{600}{\meter\per\second} $\Delta V$ beyond lunar escape is needed \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025}.  Gravity assists, coupled with small burns or solar electric propulsion, could then return these payloads to the Moon with flyby speeds around \SIrange{10}{20}{\kilo\meter\per\second}.

If these payloads are PuffSats filled with lunar volatiles, they could launch several times their own mass from the Moon. This option requires mastering lunar ISRU and will likely need some initial fuel supplies from Earth. However, it offers faster launch capacity growth than PuffSat cycles that use suborbital rockets launched from Earth. This approach also avoids the need to master the prograde/retrograde chamber collisions discussed in \autoref{sec:lunar_rockets_no_fuel}.

Near-Earth asteroids and comets are other possible sources of volatiles for PuffSats, but they have serious disadvantages. Mining in low gravity with limited available energy is difficult.  The logistics of resupply and navigating materials into Earth flyby trajectories are also challenging. The largest easily-reached near-Earth objects have smaller volatile inventories than the Moon, even without utilizing lunar oxygen (see \autoref{sec:lunar_mining}). The Moon is therefore the best initial target for PuffSat ISRU.

\section{One Hiroshima Per Second. The World Set Free}\label{sec:world_set_free}
In \autoref{sec:no_isru_rocket}, we propelled PuffSats to \SI{150}{\kilo\meter\per\second} at \SI{1}{\astronomicalunit} using solar gravity and pulsed propulsion. A \SI{60}{\tera\watt} pulsed heat source, which is equivalent to one Hiroshima bomb per second \cite{hiroshima}, requires harnessing the kinetic energy of just \SI{5.3}{\tonne\per\second} of propulsion mass at these speeds. For context, humanity's total power consumption is approximately \SI{20}{\tera\watt} \cite{owid-energy-production-consumption}. Given that typical power plants are roughly one-third efficient \cite{eia_plant_efficiency}, a single one of these pulsed heat sources could meet the energy needs of all human civilization.  This concept echoes H.G. Wells's 1913 novel \textit{The World Set Free}, where he imagined atomic bombs as a ``blazing continual explosion'' \cite{wells1914world}.  

Using kinetic projectiles to generate terrestrial power only makes sense if it achieves massive economies of scale that outperform the linear costs and land use efficiency of conventional renewables.  Let's explore how to build such an ambitious megastructure, and then explain why it's economically attractive in \autoref{sec:strawway_economics}.     

\subsection{Straw Ways To Heaven}\label{sec:straw_way_to_heaven}
The most common proposal for utilizing space-based power on Earth involves wireless power transfer from orbiting power plants to terrestrial rectenna arrays \cite{malaviya2022comprehensive}.  Unfortunately, transmitting power over such distances requires breakthroughs in beam focusing \cite{space_beaming_power} and large radiators for dissipating heat. The required transmission infrastructure, both in space and on Earth, would be colossal.  Given these challenges, let's investigate terrestrial solutions that directly convert kinetic impact energy to electricity on Earth.

To deliver kinetic energy projectiles to Earth, the projectiles must be able to withstand atmospheric entry.  One solution is to build a vacuum tube, extending from Earth's surface to space, precisely aligned to allow incoming projectiles to pass through unimpeded.  The tube is suspended from an anchor point as depicted in \autoref{fig:straw_way_to_heaven}.   Enthusiasts have proposed numerous physically plausible structures that could support an anchor hoisted above Earth's atmosphere. These include Lofstrom Launch Loops \cite{lofstrom_loop}, tethered rings \cite{swan2023tethered_ring}, global orbital rings \cite{birch1982orbital_ring}, space elevators \cite{pearson1975orbital_tower}, and space fountains \cite{hyde1985space_fountain}; for an accessible overview see also \cite{isaac_arthur_megastructures_playlist}.  However, these ambitious ideas generally suffer from very low Technology Readiness Levels (TRLs), meaning they are far from practical implementation.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{images/Straw Way To Heaven.png}
    \caption{Vacuum tube suspended from cables allows projectiles through the atmosphere}
    \label{fig:straw_way_to_heaven}
\end{figure}


Fortunately, PuffSat pulsed propulsion offers a promising path to position an anchor point above Earth. While this technology is at a low TRL, we would have already developed it to high reliability if we were using it to direct projectiles at Earth using the techniques from \autoref{sec:no_isru_rocket}.  

A heavy-lift launch vehicle such as SpaceX's Super Heavy \cite{starship} delivers the anchor assembly and its attached vacuum tube to the target altitude.   We then indefinitely hover the anchor point using equal mass PuffSats that collide their gas output head-on in a pulsed propulsion chamber (described in \autoref{sec:lunar_rockets_no_fuel}), redirecting thrust downward. The propulsion chamber exhaust can be angled slightly to provide thrust vectoring to counter atmospheric winds on the vacuum tube and support cables. From this elevated anchor point, we sequentially deploy tube segments and support cables downward until reaching the ground. A reaction control system on the anchor point adjusts the pulsed propulsion chamber so it stays precisely positioned for the next PuffSat collision.  If these PuffSats meet at $v_p=\SI{11}{\kilo\meter\per\second}$ at an average mass flow rate of \SI{1}{\tonne\per\second}, they can theoretically suspend more than \SI{1100}{\tonne}.   

In reality, energy losses would reduce thrust.   For reliable operations, we would need to lift redundant anchor points and vacuum tubes.  Together, redundancy and energy losses imply we will need additional PuffSat propulsion collision mass per second.  To keep the vacuum tube aloft, PuffSat propulsion must remain continuously active even when incoming projectiles cannot enter the tube to generate energy. Alternatively, for maintenance, the tube could be lowered at night and then re-hoisted by a heavy-lift rocket the following day.  Compared to magnetic systems like Lofstrom Launch Loops, hoisting an anchor with rockets or PuffSat pulsed propulsion is energy inefficient. That inefficiency is acceptable here. Lifting a few tons per second of propulsion PuffSat mass from Earth (as in \autoref{sec:starship_safelaunch}) or the moon (as in \autoref{sec:lunar_rockets_no_fuel}) seems economically viable if we use our vacuum tube to cleanly produce all the energy required by humanity.

\subsection{Vacuum Tube Details}\label{sec:vacuum_tube_details}
To mitigate micrometeorite risk in the upper atmosphere, the vacuum tube incorporates sacrificial shielding or modular, swappable segments. Self-healing materials \cite{self_healing_material_survey} may repair small tube ruptures.  For example, a thin wire mesh embedded in the tube wall could detect conductivity changes caused by tears or punctures. Briefly increasing the current through these wires would cause localized heating, melting the surrounding material and heat-sealing small breaches. Redundant parallel tubes may also be deployed to ensure continuous operation despite localized damage.  

The tube's walls get thinner at higher altitudes, where less structural support is needed to maintain a vacuum.  At lower heights, ribbed or corrugated designs  keep the tube lightweight and flexible. Thin, high-strength tensile cables attached every few meters along the tube connect the tube and the anchor point.  Each attachment point includes several parallel tensile cables, ensuring structural redundancy in the event that one or more are severed by micrometeorite impacts.   Equipped with winching micro-actuators, these cables dynamically control their length to precisely tune the tube's curvature. The actuators shape the tube to counteract Coriolis, tidal, drag, and gravity-variation forces affecting the projectile's transit. The anchor point itself will rotate throughout the day to track the Sun.  Sensors along the vacuum tube measure and broadcast the tube's position and shape, allowing incoming projectiles to navigate through it accurately.

Additional sensors are placed along the cables and at the top of the tube to accurately measure any perturbations. These measurements help to precisely model the projectile's trajectory as it enters the tube. Once a projectile is inside, making adjustments with microthrusters would contaminate the vacuum and damage the walls. Instead, if the tube is metalized or partially conductive, we can steer projectiles using small magnets. These magnets generate Lorentz forces from induced currents in the walls, allowing for precise control. In its upper, near-orbital reaches the wall also meets atomic oxygen, which erodes bare polymer, and unfiltered ultraviolet. A continuous metallized or silica coating holds off both, the same technique that protects polyimide solar-array blankets in orbit \cite{ao_protection_banks}, and the metallized version doubles as the conductive layer the magnetic steering relies on.

Creating and maintaining a high vacuum in long tunnels is no simple task, but facilities like the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) prove it's feasible. Our tube, like the LHC's, uses non-evaporable getter (NEG) \cite{neg_coatings} thin-film coatings placed at intervals to chemically scavenge most atmospheric gases. These coatings, however, are ineffective against noble gases like argon. While we could embed cryopumps throughout the tube, that would add complexity and mass. Fortunately, when our projectiles collide with residual argon atoms, the atoms rebound at extreme velocities.  At these speeds, argon atoms become implanted a few nanometers deep into vacuum tube walls.  However, these impacts also sputter atoms from both the walls and the projectiles. The NEG coatings scavenge sputtered volatile atoms, while non-volatile elements recondense onto the tube interior.  Together, NEG coatings and argon implantation preserve vacuum integrity despite atomic sputtering. To ensure an even better vacuum inside the tube, thin films of oxygen-specific chemical adsorbents  are applied to the top few kilometers. By limiting exospheric oxygen from building up in the tube, we make our vacuum comparable to low orbit environments.  Scheduled tube refurbishment or replacement will be necessary due to wear from micrometeorites, argon sputtering, and NEG chemical consumption. Even if we pessimistically need a replacement once per month, the costs are minor compared to the revenue from global power generation.

Projectiles that catastrophically miss the vacuum tube burn up high in the atmosphere; their extreme velocity guarantees disintegration well above the ground, producing only a Chelyabinsk-style airburst \cite{popova2021chelyabinsk} rather than a surface impact.   Assuming our vacuum tubes are in remote areas, ground-level effects on populated regions are limited.   The vacuum tube itself is likely destroyed, but redundant vacuum tubes would then be moved into place.

\subsection{Tether Tapering And Countering Extreme Drag Forces}\label{sec:tether_tapering}

Incoming projectiles experience dynamic pressure approximately  $\frac{v_{\text{projectile}}^2}{v_{\text{PuffSat}}^2} = \frac{(150\,\text{km/s})^2}{(11\,\text{km/s})^2} \approx 185$ times greater than that faced by trans-lunar PuffSats, due to their much higher velocity. This amplifies drag forces far beyond those analyzed in \autoref{sec:estimate_cold_gas}.

Because these projectiles spend only \SIrange{1}{2}{\second} at lower altitudes when arriving at such extreme velocities, we may be able to counteract the resulting terminal forces by briefly firing small chemical thrusters and vectoring their thrust. Despite the rarefied air at \SI{200}{\kilo\meter} altitude, projectiles still face intense ram heating. Thin, thermally conductive skins can be cooled via convective heat transfer and latent heat absorption from phase-changing PuffSat volatiles (e.g., water vaporization).

Projectile guidance and thermal management are easier if projectiles enter the vacuum tube at \SIrange{300}{400}{\kilo\meter} altitude, rather than the \SI{200}{\kilo\meter} baseline used in \autoref{sec:formation_challenges_current_missions}. That altitude exceeds the self-support breaking length of Kevlar or SK99 Dyneema, so the column wants PBO, whose \SI{380}{\kilo\meter} self-support length is the highest of the candidates \cite{zylon_technical_info}. The taper of the following paragraphs then supplies the safety margin (see \autoref{sec:fiber_selection}). A continuous silica or metallized barrier holds off the atomic oxygen and ultraviolet the exposed column meets, and the dry vacuum keeps PBO's hydrolysis pathway shut, so PBO earns its place in the coated column rather than the humid air near the surface.

Unlike the projectiles, PuffSats that provide pulsed propulsion to anchor the tube travel at only \SI{11}{\kilo\meter\per\second}. Their lower velocity enables more precise navigation and impact targeting at reduced altitudes. Therefore, we can place one anchor at the lower \SI{200}{\kilo\meter} altitude and connect the upper portion of the tube to a second anchor at higher altitude. Shorter cables anchored to surface vessels can serve as guy wires to counter winds on the lower \SI{20}{\kilo\meter} of the tube. These guy wires can be actively winched to adjust tension, minimizing peak wind forces the longer anchor cables must withstand.

Despite these optimizations, our current cable designs still lack sufficient engineering safety margin. Tapered cables offer a promising solution. The mass-optimal cross-sectional area of a tapered cable at distance $x$ from the anchor scales as $A(x) = C e^{-\frac{x}{b}}$, where $b$ is the material's breaking length and $C$ is a constant (see \autoref{sec:breaking_length_derived}). 

To maintain an engineering safety factor of 3 across a cable stretched to its full breaking length, the cross-sectional area at the anchor must be roughly 20 times greater than at the point where the cable connects to the lowest portions of the tube. Fortunately, this tapering requirement aligns with our existing structural strategy. We already deploy thin control wires every few meters along the tube. By progressively merging and intertwining these wires near anchor altitudes, we achieve the required cross-sectional increase without adding appreciable mass beyond our existing budget.



\subsection{Power Plant Chamber Details}
Beneath the vacuum tube, we propose excavating a steam chamber designed to vaporize incoming projectiles. These chambers are large construction projects, engineered to withstand sudden changes in pressure and temperature.  The chamber walls would be lined with steel backed by bunker-grade reinforced concrete, while internally mounted spring-loaded metal plates would absorb shock and mitigate structural stress.  

Suppose a \SI{0.1}{\cubic\kilo\meter} steam chamber containing \SI{50}{\kilo\gram\per\cubic\meter} steam receives a \SI{600}{\tera\joule} kinetic impact.   As a simplification, let's say the steam has a constant specific heat of \SI{3.5}{\kilo\joule\per(\kilo\gram\celsius)}.  In this case the temperature change is 
$\frac{\SI{600}{\tera\joule}}{\SI{5e9}{\kilo\gram}\times\SI{3500}{\joule\per(\kilo\gram\kelvin)}} = \SI{34.2}{\celsius}$.  An impact of this magnitude every 10 seconds produces the heat to continuously provide all humanity's energy.  This steam chamber would be connected to thousands of turbines and a massive cooling loop to harness and dissipate this energy.  

Releasing large amounts of heat into the atmosphere would lead to unacceptable local temperature increases. Using a coolant loop that exchanges heat with deep ocean water will also have environmental consequences. Spreading $\SI{40}{\tera\watt}$ of heat over $\SI{10}{\cubic\kilo\meter}$ of deep ocean water would increase its temperature by $\frac{\SI{40}{\tera\watt}\cdot\SI{3600}{\second\per\hour}}{\SI{1e13}{\kilo\gram}\cdot\SI{4186}{\joule\per\kilo\gram\per\celsius}} = \SI{3.44}{\celsius\per\hour}$.  This rapid temperature change could severely damage local marine habitats.

The mid-water column is not empty. Mesopelagic fish alone may total around 10 billion tonnes, likely the bulk of all fish biomass \cite{irigoien2014mesopelagic}. The case for tolerable harm rests instead on scale. The directly heated volume is a vanishingly small fraction of the ocean, so the disturbance stays local rather than basin-wide. When compared to the widespread impacts of global anthropogenic climate change or even the wildlife displacement caused by solar farms, the environmental harm from this specific heat rejection method appears relatively modest.

The projectiles would have explosive or water interiors that disintegrate quickly on hypervelocity shock contact with the chamber's high pressure steam. Made from carbon composites and polymers, their structural exteriors would vaporize into gases at impact temperatures. Inside the steam chamber, temperatures are so high that the carbon turns into a superheated plasma. Steam also decomposes in these conditions, releasing oxygen that combusts this carbon along with any air present. Although this process forms very little carbon soot before the carbon turns to carbon dioxide, any that does form can be filtered out with charcoal screens before reaching the turbines.   The remaining gases are released or sequestered as they travel along with the steam cycling through the system.   Solid residues that precipitate on chamber walls could be cleaned off either continuously or during maintenance windows.  Continuous processes might rely on trapping solids in cheap viscous liquids like silicone fluids, organic oils, or molten salts that slowly ooze down chamber walls and drain at the bottom.  

An alternative converter skips the steam. If the chamber floor is held at vacuum, each projectile arrives as fast plasma that a magnetic channel can decelerate and tap for current directly, the way a magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) generator draws power from moving conductive gas \cite{dominguez2024mhd}. This favors many rapid, small impulses over a few large ones, since a smaller pulse is easier to contain and clears before the next arrives.

\subsection{Vacuum Exchange And Airlock Details}\label{sec:vacuum_airlock}
An airlock separates the steam chamber from the rest of the tube as shown in \autoref{fig:power_plant_vacuum_exchange}.   A door with a closing speed of \SI{300}{\meter\per\second} could close a \SI{30}{\centi\meter} diameter aperture in \SI{1}{\milli\second}.  Even a \SI{5}{\meter} long airlock is long enough to prevent steam molecules from traversing the airlock in this short time.  

\begin{figure}[htpb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{images/Power Plant Cooling and Generators.png}
    \caption{Diagram of the power plant and vacuum airlock system; new airlock segments are rotated in between impacts.}
    \label{fig:power_plant_vacuum_exchange}
\end{figure}

Designing an airtight door that can open and close at the necessary speeds is beyond current technology. However, a system of rapidly rotating disks with strategically placed apertures can create a quick-closing, leaky seal.

This system works like this:

\begin{itemize}
\item Two or more pressure-resistant disks with identical radii of perhaps \SI{10}{\meter} are closely mounted on an axial shaft.

\item These disks spin at slightly different speeds, and each has a \SI{30}{\centi\meter} aperture near their outer edge.

\item We add mass along the radial line of each disk's aperture to keep the rotating disks balanced.

\item When the apertures don't overlap, they block the vacuum tube and prevent steam from entering.

\item The apertures align every few seconds over the airlock opening.   This gives us time to close a second airtight door over the airlock opening.

\item The disk apertures may align more frequently than projectile arrivals.  Since projectiles might only arrive every \SI{10}{\second}, it's tricky to design fast spinning disks that only overlap at this low frequency.  As long as the secondary door can close between alignments, and the alignments are synchronized with projectile arrivals, the airlock system meets its operational requirements.
\end{itemize}

This rotating disk system offloads handling the pressure differential between steam and vacuum.  That simplifies the design for the separate, high-performance airtight door that seals the vacuum completely. The airtight door is still fast, closing on the order of a few hundred milliseconds.  Before this door closes, steam leaks through the clearance between the disks and the airlock.  This clearance is likely at least a millimeter since we must handle vibrations.  To further mitigate steam ingress, chemical desiccants are embedded in the disk housing and airlock walls. Thin films of desiccants are also used along the first kilometer or so of the vacuum tube.  The disk housing and airlock walls might also be cooled to freeze or at least slow steam molecules.

At the bottom of the airlock, the projectile releases enormous heat on contact with steam. Asteroid air-burst simulations suggest most of this energy is directed in the projectile's direction of travel, away from the airlock and the closest chamber walls \cite{tunguska_downwards}. If still needed to handle the high initial heat, sacrificial plastic segments can form the bottom part of the airlock pipe. These plastics should be strong at low temperatures but vaporize easily to avoid creating shrapnel. Their brief exposure to steam before projectile impact can be buffered by a coat of ice, which forestalls heating. To prevent the production of highly toxic gases when they decompose, the plastic should not be halogenated. Cheap polyethylene pipes may satisfy all these requirements.  The pipe can be ribbed for strength and corrugated to stop buckling under the steam's pressure.  These techniques reduce the pipe mass needed to withstand the steam's compressive load.  A thicker plastic plug at the bottom of the pipe could drastically quicken projectile disintegration, especially if the projectile primarily contains materials like water or explosives. The projectile's enormous kinetic energy would pulverize it on plug impact. The resulting intense heat and shock wave breaks any solid debris into smaller shards that rapidly vaporize in the steam far from chamber walls.  An added benefit of these sacrificial segments is that they lengthen the time the airlock door has to seal before steam can travel far enough to enter the vacuum tube.

A new airlock segment is swapped in between kinetic projectile impacts, providing time to restore the high vacuum offline before its next use. Sacrificial bottom sections are also replaced and reattached while the main airlock segment is out of rotation.

\subsection{With Economies Of Scale, Our Ambitions Can Scale Too}\label{sec:strawway_economics}

While a global power plant requires a massive initial capital investment, its costs scale favorably with output. The cost of suspending a large vacuum tube depends very little on the plant's generating capacity. The cost of deep-sea cooling loops would also be amortized across the entire planetary-scale facility.  A multi-terawatt installation could achieve per-watt cost reductions through bulk procurement of thousands of identical turbine and generator units. 

Mass production reduces the cost of projectile electronics and microthrusters. The cost of these components per projectile also remains nearly the same as individual projectile size increases.  Ten seconds of planetary-scale power corresponds to $\SI{20}{\tera\watt} \times \SI{10}{\second} \approx \SI{5.56e7}{\kilo\watt\hour} = \$555,556$ at 1 cent per kilowatt-hour.  This energy revenue would likely dwarf the material costs of the projectiles themselves, especially if their interiors are mostly water or explosives.      

Power from our plant could be globally distributed by supertanker-sized batteries that connect to urban power grids at coastal ports.  While the cost of utility-scale batteries is progressively declining, it remains a hurdle. Current battery designs prioritize efficient energy storage and release. However, if energy efficiency becomes less critical, we could design cheaper low-performance batteries. Given that our power source exhibits strong cost efficiencies, we could simply increase the power output to compensate for any energy losses incurred by using these more affordable batteries for distribution. 

Fossil fuels remain entrenched as the dominant energy source, even as cleaner alternatives like renewables gain traction. High-temperature heat from low-cost projectile impacts offers a novel route for synthesizing hydrocarbon fuels directly from water and carbon dioxide.

One promising pathway is the cerium oxide cycle \cite{CeriaThermoCycles}, where cerium oxide is reduced at high temperatures and low pressures. Operating at low pressures simplifies vacuum chamber and airlock design (see \autoref{sec:vacuum_tube_details}). The reduced cerium oxide then reacts with carbon dioxide and water to produce carbon monoxide and hydrogen. These are precursors for hydrocarbon synthesis via industrially mature chemical processes \cite{water_gas_shift}.

An alternative approach, adapted from chemical looping in blast furnaces \cite{iron_chemical_looping}, uses iron to reduce carbon dioxide to carbon monoxide. This carbon monoxide can then be combined with water to produce fuels through standard chemical pathways \cite{fischer_tropsch}. The resulting iron oxide may be thermally decomposed to regenerate iron, forming a closed-loop cycle. Iron, being denser than iron oxide, naturally separates under gravity, much like the stratification of slag and molten iron during steel production. Incoming projectiles can be designed to gasify just prior to impact, allowing energy deposition without direct mechanical disruption. At velocities around \SI{150}{\kilo\meter\per\second}, low-density gas atoms penetrate a few nanometers into the lava, transferring heat through shallow kinetic interactions. This reduces shock propagation and ejecta formation. To further enhance coupling, finely divided iron oxide powder can be injected into the vacuum chamber, presenting a much larger effective surface area to the incoming hot gas. The powder absorbs and redistributes the energy before it settles back into the molten pool. The chamber walls remain protected from peak loads by the intervening lava, which is both a volumetric heat sink and a radiative buffer, damping transient spikes.

Both cerium and iron oxides decompose only under very low pressures, requiring aggressive removal of oxygen from the reaction environment. An alternative approach is to use water-splitting cycles, such as the sulfur–iodine or copper–chlorine processes, to generate hydrogen \cite{sulfur_iodine_chlorine}. Hydrogen, together with carbon dioxide, forms an ideal feedstock for hydrocarbon synthesis. Although these water-splitting cycles run cooler than direct thermolysis, the sulfur--iodine acid-decomposition step still operates near \SI{850}{\celsius} and exposes reactor walls to hot sulfuric acid. SiC-based ceramics, including silicon carbide matrix composites, are an established candidate liner material for this stage, combining corrosion resistance with the thermal-shock and fracture tolerance needed under pulsed operation.  These liners could coat a shock-absorbing nickel superalloy chamber \cite{inconel_718_strong}, which is externally heated by a molten bath of lava or liquid iron.  Projectile impact energy keeps the lava molten, indirectly providing the steady high temperatures that are optimal for the reaction chamber. This configuration also shields the ceramics from direct impact shocks. 

By bypassing costly steam turbines and simplifying vacuum tube maintenance, this direct-heating approach could enable viable hydrocarbon synthesis before large-scale electricity generation becomes cost-effective.

Though the power plant and distribution infrastructure would cost billions to construct and operate, a planetary-scale plant would make trillions annually.  Turns out, if \textit{Superman} wants to be as rich as \textit{Lex Luthor}, he should get limitless energy not from the light of a yellow sun, but from its gravity \cite{superman2025}.

\subsection{Comparison To Project PACER}
Project PACER was a 1970s Los Alamos concept to generate power by extracting steam from nuclear bombs detonated in a salt cavern \cite{pacer_1975}.   Later improvements protected the cavern walls with an artificial waterfall of tritium breeding liquid salts \cite{pacer_revisited}.  

Unlike PACER, our proposal does not produce radioactive waste or encourage the mass production of nuclear weapons.   Should a liquid wall be needed to protect the chamber, it can be made from cheap materials like water.  Tritium breeding and extraction are not required.   Our design eliminates the need for a biological shield, which simplifies the direct coupling of steam to turbines. This direct steam cycle enables the rapid heat exchange with the environment necessary to build terawatt-scale power plants.

\subsection{To Solve Climate Change, Make The Earth A \textit{Death Star}}\label{sec:death_star}
A \textit{Death Star} \cite{death_star} is a giant sphere with a narrow 2-meter opening where you can shoot something down to create an explosion inside the main reactor.   Building a \textit{Death Star} from scratch  would be a huge undertaking.   Fortunately, if we build the Straw Way To Heaven described in \autoref{sec:straw_way_to_heaven}, we're living on one.  Although our Straw Way generator produces clean energy from incoming projectiles, its power production is restricted to daytime hours when the straw can face the sun.   Also, a limitless clean energy source would only slow climate change.   Industrial carbon emissions from steel, cement, agriculture, and other processes would still heat the planet \cite{steel_and_cement}.   

\subsubsection{That's No Moon .  Oh Wait, It's Literally Our Moon}\cite{kenobi1977moon}
Projectiles made from lunar materials could enter a terrestrial Straw Way from high angles of attack around the clock.  To generate \SI{60}{\tera\watt} of heat from lunar bombardment, we'd need an astonishing \SI{1}{\kilo\tonne\per\second} of lunar mass to pass through the terrestrial vacuum tube, since lunar material arrives far slower than the \SI{150}{\kilo\meter\per\second} solar projectiles and so carries far less kinetic energy per kilogram. Such a vast quantity is daunting, but the momentum for launching off the moon could come from kinetic projectiles inbound from the Sun (as described in \autoref{sec:no_isru_rocket}). Beyond providing thrust, these projectiles aimed at the Moon could vaporize lunar regolith. The resulting thermal energy could be harnessed for electricity generation or to power chemical and industrial processes directly. This lunar power source could support large-scale mining and production of the oxygen gas used in PuffSat pulsed propulsion  (as discussed in \autoref{sec:lunar_mining}).

Lunar regolith is primarily carbon-depleted rock \cite{mckay1991lunar}. After the regolith-derived impactors heat steam to drive a global-scale generator, the process yields a waste column of hot regolith and scalding water. If this waste is channeled through thermally insulated pipes into deep ocean water, the intense hydrostatic pressure compresses the regolith. Its large surface area and elevated temperature accelerate chemical reactions that convert silicates into carbonates, pulling dissolved carbon dioxide from seawater \cite{seifritz1990silicates}. Deep ocean pressure further shifts chemical equilibrium toward carbonate formation \cite{kelemen2008peridotite}. With an incoming regolith flow of $\SI{1}{\kilo\tonne\per\second} \approx \SI{31}{\giga\tonne\per\year}$, we could mineralize billions of tons of dissolved carbon dioxide annually. This would enhance the ocean's capacity to absorb atmospheric carbon dioxide, making lunar mineral sequestration a viable strategy to offset most of the world's industrial carbon emissions.



\subsection{Fusion Propulsion And Epstein Drives}\label{sec:epstein_drives}
The fast projectiles from \autoref{sec:no_isru_rocket} could propel a spacecraft around the inner solar system at relatively high speeds.   As our technology advances, we'd want to travel on brachistochrone trajectories under constant Earth gravity acceleration between the planets as envisioned in \textit{The Expanse} \cite{Corey2012Drive}.  Evaporative cooling, paired with high-reflectivity coatings inspired by NASA's yttrium-oxide ``solar white'' work for cryo-tank thermal control \cite{nasa_solar_white_y2o3}, could in principle enable spacecraft to approach the Sun to 2 solar radii. That's closer than the Parker Solar Probe's 9.86 solar radii \cite{nasa2018parker_heat}.  At this close solar periapsis, prograde and retrograde projectile collision velocities would exceed \SI{869}{\kilo\meter\per\second} \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025} in the reference frame of a fusion pulsed propulsion chamber on the target rocket.

A 1979 Los Alamos Impact Fusion workshop concluded speeds of just \SI{200}{\kilo\meter\per\second} might be sufficient for precompressed gram sized fusion fuel projectiles to ignite \cite{impactfusion1979}.   Larger projectiles would likely be easier to confine long enough for high fusion energy gain.   A lead tamper surrounds a few hundred grams of fusion fuel, as depicted in \autoref{fig:fusion_tamper}. This arrangement could ignite fusion explosions and trigger immense exhaust velocities.  Shocked lead would reach even higher temperatures than the fuel because its high atomic number implies greater energy per atom (see \autoref{sec:radiative_differences}).  Therefore, lead or another high-Z material is centrally embedded in the fusion fuel at the tip of the colliding masses. This embedded lead acts as a ``spark plug'' \cite{morland1979hbomb} for fusion, analogous to the spark plug in Teller-Ulam style \cite{doe_hbomb_history} nuclear explosions. Pulsed fusion propulsion would likely employ the shaped nuclear charges Project Orion explored to redirect energy and thrust.  
With mostly aneutronic fusion fuel, projectiles could reach velocities on the order of thousands of kilometers per second.  Aneutronic fusion directly converts energy into charged particle kinetic energy, bypassing the mass penalty of neutron shielding.  A seed of deuterium-tritium could ignite a larger deuterium and helium-3 mixture to achieve aneutronic fusion rocket speeds.   As the spacecraft accelerates, it can also reach speeds where it triggers fusion by crashing into slower moving fusion fuel targets.   If carefully planned, these chains of fast-moving projectiles could create interplanetary highways, allowing people to travel between planets under comfortable artificial Earth gravity in time frames comparable to ocean liners crossing the Atlantic.  

\subsection{The Sixth Question For Climate Change}\label{sec:sith_question}
Bill Gates' excellent book, \textit{How to Avoid a Climate Disaster} \cite{gates2021avoid}, details five practical questions \cite{breakthroughenergy_2021_five} any effective climate solution must address. Our Straw Way To Heaven (see \autoref{sec:straw_way_to_heaven}) addresses Gates' questions better than other alternatives. It zeroes carbon emissions, reverses cement emissions, and supplies indefinite global power at marginal costs that decline with output.

Yet I propose a sixth question that could rally public support for a particular climate solution. Beyond just reducing our electric bills and helping the environment, how will this breakthrough improve daily life? An investment on the scale required to solve climate change should grant human society new abilities, such as hypervelocity transport and affordable space travel. PuffSat pulsed propulsion can achieve all of these things.  

\section{When We Get Greedy, We'll Go to Phoebe}\label{sec:greedy_phoebe}
In \autoref{sec:no_isru_rocket} we discussed using Sun-grazing Oberth maneuvers to accelerate projectiles to \SI{150}{\kilo\meter\per\second} at \SI{1}{\astronomicalunit}. These high velocities enable the large $\Delta V$ required to place the next suborbital rocket into prograde and retrograde colliding orbits near the Sun.  However, as detailed in \autoref{sec:periapsis_challenges}, such close solar approaches introduce severe engineering and operational challenges, increasing projectile costs compared to less extreme solar encounters.  Suborbital rockets used to launch from Earth might also produce unacceptable levels of pollution in the upper atmosphere.

Orbits with higher apoapsis require less $\Delta V$ to shed orbital angular momentum and lower their periapsis. For example, transferring to a solar impact trajectory from Saturn requires only about \SI{10}{\kilo\meter\per\second}, compared to roughly \SI{30}{\kilo\meter\per\second} from Earth \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025}. This makes Saturn a more efficient departure point for reaching the inner solar system or the Sun.

Saturn's irregular moon Phoebe lies just \SI{6.6}{\degree} from the solar ecliptic plane (its orbital inclination of \SI{173.4}{\degree} falls \SI{6.6}{\degree} short of a perfectly retrograde \SI{180}{\degree}) \cite{phoebe}, giving it a favorable alignment for interplanetary transfers. Its high-altitude orbit around Saturn yields a low orbital velocity, enabling efficient low $\Delta V$ transfers to elliptical trajectories that match Phoebe's apoapsis and dip toward Saturn at periapsis.  From Phoebe, a prograde rocket could be launched to collide with retrograde payloads also originating from Phoebe. These collisions could take place in a pulsed propulsion chamber near Saturn, enabling highly efficient Oberth maneuvers. With careful targeting to avoid Saturn's rings, this method could launch spacecraft toward the inner solar system. Precisely aiming such collisions near Saturn is challenging, but likely easier than attempting similar maneuvers at the extreme conditions of close solar periapsis described in \autoref{sec:no_isru_rocket}. PuffSats launched from Saturn toward Earth could power a Straw Way generator (see \autoref{sec:straw_way_to_heaven}) or deliver suborbital rockets into orbit (see \autoref{sec:starship_safelaunch}).

These rockets could also return to Phoebe at high speeds, launching additional mass toward Saturn via pulsed propulsion. This bootstrapped launch cycle from Phoebe mirrors the lunar exponential growth loop discussed in \autoref{sec:lunar_rockets_no_fuel}, but benefits both from Phoebe's lower escape velocity and from Saturn's larger gravitational Oberth effect. Power for mining operations on Phoebe could come from solar energy concentrated using lenses made from Phoebe's native ice or locally produced plastic. Fission reactors transported from Earth are another possibility, as is thermal energy generated by returning impactors.  One key advantage kinetic impact energy offers is that its power doesn't decline quadratically with solar distance, unlike solar panels.

With a bulk density consistent with roughly 50\% ice by mass \cite{castillo_rogez2012_phoebe} and surface ice mapped by Cassini \cite{phoebe}, Phoebe offers nearly unlimited volatile resources to fill PuffSats. Unlike the Moon's permanently shadowed ice deposits, Phoebe's ice-rich regions receive direct sunlight. Although faint, this illumination enables solar concentrators to generate power directly adjacent to mining operations. Phoebe's abundant water could be used to wash regolith from equipment, mitigating the mechanical fouling that plagues lunar hardware. Its regolith, composed largely of frozen volatiles, is likely softer and less abrasive than the Moon's harsh dust, reducing wear on machinery. By contrast, lunar volatiles are scarce, and oxygen extraction from lunar regolith (see \autoref{sec:lunar_mining}) is highly energy-intensive.

Phoebe's ultra-low gravity makes it difficult to anchor mining infrastructure securely. However, its volatile-rich composition allows another approach: retro-rockets powered by in-situ steam or chemical propellants manufactured from Phoebe's volatiles. These thrusters press equipment against the surface and absorb the recoil of mining and launch operations, supplying the hold-down force that gravity cannot. To save energy, icy reaction mass could also be launched mechanically from Phoebe's surface without first converting it to steam. If Phoebe's gravity proves too low for certain operations, Iapetus could be a backup site. Similarly, Jupiter's moon Himalia may support analogous maneuvers if exploration reveals sizable volatile deposits.

Mining moons like Phoebe to supply PuffSat payloads is easier than extracting conventional raw materials for Earth delivery. PuffSats execute high-velocity flybys, eliminating the need for costly deceleration maneuvers or bulky reentry shielding near Earth. And unlike ice or gold, the ultra-high-energy-density pulses generated by PuffSat impacts are irreproducible. No known terrestrial technology, short of nuclear weapons, can produce such large-scale, high-density energy bursts.

\subsection{Mining Helium-3} \label{sec:mining_helium_3}
Helium-3 for aneutronic fusion may be available on the Earth's moon \cite{wittenberg1986lunar_he3}, but likely not in quantities to support large-scale fast interplanetary transport as discussed in \autoref{sec:epstein_drives}.   However, the gas giants contain vast supplies of helium-3 \cite{palaszewski2005atmospheric}.   A nuclear fission thermal rocket or scramjet could extract helium-3 and hydrogen while flying in Saturn's atmosphere and then jump a few thousand kilometers above the atmosphere.  It could then separate from a payload carrying mined helium-3, falling back to Saturn to mine more.  An accident on such a fission rocket would cause no harm, because the heavy fission byproducts would sink to Saturn's core, with no surface to contaminate. Propulsion PuffSats from Phoebe could then send the helium-3 payload into the inner solar system for use in fusion rockets.  

If fission rockets prove politically unpalatable, chemical rockets may also work.   Chemical rocket fuel powers a motor in Saturn's atmosphere for the energy to mine helium-3.  The rocket then fires its engines to hop above Saturn's atmosphere.   Propulsion PuffSats from Phoebe push the rocket into a stable orbit where it is refueled and releases its helium-3 payload.  Then new propulsion PuffSats decelerate the refueled rocket so that it falls into Saturn's atmosphere for another mining round.

\subsection{A Ceres-ly Good Alternative}\label{sec:ceresly_good}
Ceres may offer a Goldilocks environment for sourcing PuffSat materials. Pre-Dawn modeling of its low bulk density put Ceres' water-ice content at roughly 17 to 27\% by mass \cite{mccord_sotin2005ceres}, and the Dawn mission later found ice within \SI{1}{\meter} of the surface at high latitudes \cite{prettyman2017dawn_ceres_water}. Near-surface deposits may also contain nearly 20\% carbon \cite{marchi2019_ceres_carbon}, enabling in-situ production of methane and other volatiles. Nitrogen-rich phyllosilicates found on Ceres are promising for fabricating micro-explosives \cite{desanctis2015_ceres_ammoniated_phyllosilicates}. In short, Ceres contains all the essential chemistry for manufacturing icy PuffSats on-site (see \autoref{sec:icy_puffsat}).

Ceres offers adequate solar illumination, gravity sufficient for mining infrastructure, shorter transit times than Phoebe, and a low escape velocity. A solar impact trajectory from Ceres requires only about \SI{18}{\kilo\meter\per\second}, considerably less than the roughly \SI{30}{\kilo\meter\per\second} required from Earth \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025}. This lower $\Delta V$ supports Ceres-based launch cycles that harness the Oberth effect near the Sun with less extreme perihelion or increased payload capacity per cycle relative to Earth-based alternatives (see \autoref{sec:no_isru_rocket}).

Achieving centimeter-accuracy PuffSat interceptions around Ceres may prove easier than in most other prime solar system locations. In high-altitude, easily accessible orbits above Ceres, PuffSats would encounter negligible unmodeled forces. Atmospheric drag is absent, magnetic interference is small, solar illumination perturbations are modest, and deviations from modeled gravity are weak. Jupiter's influence is non-negligible but predictable. 

Rather than relying on active solar tracking in the dim sunlight around Ceres' ice-rich poles, solar power depots can be stationed in polar sun-synchronous orbits. These orbital platforms maintain continuous exposure to sunlight, bypassing the thermal cycling and power interruptions caused by Ceres' night. If we make transparent plastics like acrylic or polycarbonate from Ceres' raw material, we can produce thick concentrating optics that shield photovoltaic arrays from micrometeorites and space radiation. We favor UV-stable acrylic over polycarbonate, which yellows under ultraviolet \cite{polycarbonate_uv_aging}, and vacuum-bake the optic before flight so its outgassing does not fog the array \cite{outgassing_rp1124}. Outgassing rate also falls steeply as temperature drops, following an Arrhenius dependence \cite{outgassing_temperature_decay}, so at Ceres' orbital temperatures, more than \SI{200}{\kelvin} below the \SI{125}{\celsius} screening test, a bake-dried optic sheds volatiles far more slowly. Should plastic prove too lossy over time, a fused-silica optic drawn from the same regolith avoids both, drawing on fused silica's long use as the spacecraft window material from Apollo to the ISS \cite{fused_silica_windows}.  This shielding extends solar panel lifespans without imported mass from Earth. Ceres' low gravity enables lofting mined carbon and water into orbit with minimal rocket propulsion. Once in orbit, these volatiles can be converted into fuel at solar-powered satellites.  This fuel maintains satellite orbits, enables return trips to Ceres, and powers mining operations on the surface.   We close the loop by mining more volatiles on Ceres and taking them back to the solar satellites for fuel conversion.

Converting those volatiles into fuel admits two chemistries. Methane and oxygen (methalox), both made from Ceres' carbon and water, are the workhorse and need no deep cooling. Liquid hydrogen and oxygen are the higher-performance option, and the passive sunshade from \autoref{sec:lox_puffsat} is what makes hydrogen practical this far out. A shaded radiator equilibrates with the attenuated sunlight, so its temperature falls as the inverse square root of heliocentric distance. Anchored on the JWST sunshield's \SI{40}{\kelvin} at \SI{1}{\astronomicalunit} \cite{jwst_sunshield}, that is about \SI{24}{\kelvin} at Ceres near \SI{2.77}{\astronomicalunit}. Hydrogen's critical temperature, the warmest at which any pressure still holds it liquid, is \SI{33}{\kelvin} \cite{nist_webbook_hydrogen}, so a passive shade keeps hydrogen liquid only beyond about \SI{1.5}{\astronomicalunit}. At the Moon or Mercury no shade works at any pressure, while at Ceres liquid hydrogen sits at a few bar. The perihelion-lowering departure burn is made at Ceres' aphelion, with the inner leg flown ballistically or by PuffSat collision, so the hydrogen is produced and burned cold, never carried hot. That buys the higher specific impulse of hydrogen and oxygen, roughly \SI{450}{\second} against about \SI{360}{\second} for methalox \cite{sutton_rocket_propulsion}. The gain serves the local logistics rockets these operations already need: return trips, orbit maintenance, mining hold-down and retro braking, and the Saturn helium-3 miner (see \autoref{sec:mining_helium_3}). Closer to the Sun, where no shade can keep a cryogen liquid, the same stored water is electrolyzed on demand with onboard solar power. It feeds either a chemical thruster (Imperial's ICE-Cube \cite{imperial_ice_cube}) or a Hall-effect thruster (Imperial's water-electrolysis Hall thruster \cite{schwertheim2022wet_het}), the same make-it-on-demand approach as the PuffSat fine-control thrusters (see \autoref{sec:fine_control_thrusters}).

Unlike Phoebe, Ceres lacks a nearby planetary body for Oberth maneuvers. However, PuffSats returning from solar periapsis can deliver momentum for high-$\Delta V$ trajectories, either back to Earth or towards close perihelion. These returning PuffSats also offer an alternative to solar power. Their controlled impacts can be used to explosively excavate rocky overburden and transiently melt large quantities of subsurface ice, enabling easy pumping to fill new PuffSats.

\subsection{Fast Money Sound Nice?  Why Not Tap Mercury's Ice?}
Mercury harbors an estimated trillion tons of water ice at its permanently shadowed poles \cite{lawrence2013mercury_ice}, with graphite as the dominant darkening agent across its surface at a few weight percent \cite{peplowski2016mercury_carbon}. These resources enable in-situ fabrication of icy PuffSats (see \autoref{sec:icy_puffsat}), which can be filled with harvested water. Heat-resistant PuffSat skins and structural plastics could be synthesized from graphite-derived carbon and ice, forming ISRU-enabled projectiles.

PuffSats launched from Mercury into low-perihelion orbits (see \autoref{sec:no_isru_rocket}) would return at extremely high velocities, ideal for PuffSat propulsion. Mercury's proximity to the Sun offers abundant solar energy for surface operations. Its short orbital period also turns launches around far faster than at Earth or destinations beyond \SI{1}{\astronomicalunit} such as Phoebe or Ceres, supporting exponential scaling of launch capacity.

However, landing on Mercury remains challenging due to its deep position within the Sun's gravity well, which demands large deceleration \cite{benkhoff2021bepicolombo}. PuffSats approaching an incoming craft opposite its direction of motion could fully decelerate the craft by crashing into its pusher plate.

Key disadvantages include Mercury's harsh regolith, extreme thermal environment, and the high $\Delta V$ required to launch into retrograde solar trajectories. To overcome this high $\Delta V$ requirement, projectiles would collide at somewhat lower perihelion, gaining additional return velocity.  Higher return velocities provide greater kinetic energy for a retrograde solar injection from Mercury.

\subsection{An Atmosphere That \textit{Mars} Dreams of PuffSat Propulsion}\label{sec:mars_skyhook}
Despite Mars' abundant raw materials, its atmosphere makes it a less-than-ideal environment for sourcing the water ice needed to fill icy PuffSats (see \autoref{sec:icy_puffsat}). A system is needed to get the mined resources above the Martian atmosphere before PuffSat pulsed propulsion can push them on their way. One solution could be to use a rotating skyhook, similar to the Boeing/Tethers Unlimited HASTOL proposal \cite{skyhook_hastol}.

A skyhook with a tip speed of approximately $\SI{3.5}{\kilo\meter\per\second}$ could effectively match and cancel the orbital velocity of a low Mars orbit, enabling direct payload transfer. This tip-speed regime is consistent with the tapered high-modulus polyethylene (HMPE) tether architecture analyzed in the HASTOL Phase II study \cite{hastol_phase_ii}, scaled to current SK99 Dyneema fiber \cite{dyneema_sk99_datasheet}.  A spinning skyhook holds steady tension for years, the regime where polyethylene creeps, so the tether wants a creep-free fiber. PBO carries about \num{1.6} times Vectran's specific strength at the same near-zero creep \cite{zylon_technical_info}, which holds tether mass down (see \autoref{sec:fiber_selection}). A UV-opaque metallized coating handles the sunlight, atomic oxygen, and abrasive dust the tip meets at perigee, and Mars' dry environment keeps PBO's hydrolysis weakness dormant.  The skyhook itself could be reaccelerated by PuffSats returning at high speeds from solar perihelion, providing pulsed propulsion.

However, since Ceres does not require a skyhook and offers other advantages (see \autoref{sec:ceresly_good}), it appears to be a superior location for early PuffSat mining.



\section{War, Policy, And Pulsed Propulsion}
\subsection{Fusion Rockets And The Outer Space Treaty}
The Outer Space Treaty \cite{outer_space_treaty} prohibits nuclear weapons in space. The legality of pure fusion rockets like those discussed in \autoref{sec:epstein_drives} requires clarification, likely through international dialogue and new legal frameworks.  Large ``cruise ship'' vehicles traveling on brachistochrone trajectories at extreme velocities would possess kinetic energies comparable to the entire global nuclear arsenal \cite{fas_nuclear_forces}. A large cruise ship displaces on the order of \SI{1e8}{\kilo\gram} \cite{livescience_cruise_displacement}; at \SI{500}{\kilo\meter\per\second} it would carry roughly \SI{1.3e19}{\joule}, about \SI{3000}{\mega\tonne} of TNT equivalent. As such, the deployment of these systems demands fail-safe, enforceable regulatory and safety protocols.

\subsection{Hackers Must Be Prevented From Causing Catastrophic Explosions}
As mentioned in \autoref{sec:vacuum_tube_details}, the energy projectiles for the Straw Way To Heaven discussed in \autoref{sec:straw_way_to_heaven} are intentionally designed to burn up high in the atmosphere without causing damage in case of a mishap.  However, a hacker could manipulate microthrusters on the projectiles to enter the atmosphere simultaneously rather than in sequence, leading to Tunguska-sized \cite{longo2007tunguska} multi-megaton explosions.  We can mitigate this risk with standard software security practices like using memory-safe languages and authenticated communication.

\subsection{Risk Of PuffSat Sabotage}
PuffSat pulsed propulsion might be useful for launching military assets like Golden Dome \cite{crs_golden_dome} or the Brilliant Boulders \cite{brilliant_boulders} discussed on the blog related to this paper \cite{aim2024}.   The PuffSats described in \autoref{sec:lunar_rockets_no_fuel} are likely vulnerable to destruction from high-powered terrestrial lasers by a military adversary.  Nations will need to defend their payloads from sabotage by hostile actors.

\subsection{Aviation Exclusion Zone Needed For Straw Ways}
Since the Straw Way To Heaven (see \autoref{sec:straw_way_to_heaven}) is a fixed structure in the sky, we will need regulations to ban aviation in its vicinity.   This seems feasible given the Straw Way will be placed in a remote area.   The host nation must also guard the Straw Way against sabotage.


\subsection{Overflight Of Third Countries And Strategic Stability}\label{sec:overflight_politics}
A suborbital point-to-point vehicle overflies countries between its departure and destination. Sovereignty above the Kármán line near \SI{100}{\kilo\meter} is not settled in law \cite{mcdowell2018karman}, so this overflight sits in a legal gray zone. Interception is one risk. Nations have shot down civilian airliners before, as with MH17 over Ukraine in 2014 \cite{dsb_mh17}. This violated the Chicago Convention, which prohibits the use of weapons against civil aircraft in flight \cite{chicago_3bis}. A vehicle on a ballistic arc can also be misread as a missile attack by early-warning systems.

Because the intercity vehicle reaches orbital velocity before reentry (see \autoref{sec:200_mile_high}), it can avoid a sensitive region by flying the long way around the great circle. The vehicle stays on orbit through the far arc and deorbits on the approach to the destination. This costs extra flight time rather than much additional $\Delta V$, so it is the simplest way to route around a contested region. If the long way is unacceptable, the vehicle can instead change course at a waypoint to steer around the region. That course change can come from an onboard correction burn or from a second PuffSat formation positioned along the path.

Flying most of the way around the planet at orbital velocity resembles a Fractional Orbital Bombardment System \cite{salt2_fobs}, which early-warning systems treat as a threat. This is best handled through policy rather than through routing. PuffSats are launched ahead of the flight into tracked orbits, and the vehicle departs a known airport on a published schedule. Monitoring systems then have time to tell a PuffSat-propelled craft from a surprise ground launch, given good-faith cooperation backed by shared tracking and prior notice. Pre-launch notification agreements already serve this purpose for ballistic missiles \cite{us_ussr_launch_notification}.

The real goal is a policy agreement not to extend armed conflict into civilian space transit. The norm against attacking civil aircraft \cite{chicago_3bis} should extend to suborbital and orbital passenger travel, consistent with the peaceful-use principle of the Outer Space Treaty \cite{outer_space_treaty}.

\subsection{Don't Sweat the Stratosphere (But Do Watch It)}\label{sec:ozone_policy}\cite{carlson1997smallstuff}
SpaceX's Earth-to-Earth Starship (see \autoref{sec:200_mile_high}) brakes by hitting the atmosphere at orbital speed. The bow shock, the shell of compressed air ahead of the vehicle, heats the surrounding gas past several thousand kelvin and makes nitric oxide out of ordinary nitrogen and oxygen. Together, this nitric oxide and the nitrogen dioxide it forms (\ce{NO_x}) make up one of the largest ozone-depletion terms for present-day spaceflight, on par with the chlorine from solid rocket motors \cite{ryan2022rocket}. A PuffSat suborbital plane has another route. It sheds orbital velocity against a retrograde formation of reentry PuffSats (see \autoref{sec:200_mile_high}) and enters the air slowly, with a weaker shock and far less \ce{NO_x}. Those PuffSats gasify to water, nitrogen, oxygen, and carbon dioxide, so they also avoid the metal-oxide pathway flagged for reentering satellites \cite{ferreira2024ozone, barker2026megaconstellation}. The braking is not free. The reentry PuffSats are extra mass that had to be launched, and a slow entry means much less \ce{NO_x}, not zero. Removing the dominant ozone term by design is still a real gain over aerobraking.

Water is the residual worth watching. Both the suborbital rocket burn and the Starship that lofts the PuffSats inject water vapor into the stratosphere on the way up. At the scale of replacing airline travel, that input deserves monitoring. Stratospheric water mildly depletes ozone \cite{ryan2022rocket} and adds a modest greenhouse warming \cite{solomon2010stratospheric}, but it clears in a few years, up to about a decade for the highest injections \cite{hunga2026residence}. That is far below the roughly \SIrange{50}{100}{\year} atmospheric lifetimes of the CFCs \cite{wmo2022ozone} and the centuries over which carbon dioxide persists \cite{ipcc2021ar6}. The water the PuffSats themselves release sits near \SI{200}{\kilo\meter}, well above the \SIrange{15}{35}{\kilo\meter} ozone layer. There it photodissociates into hydrogen and hydroxyl, and the light hydrogen escapes to space, so most of it never reaches the ozone below, though some may. The short residence time and the avoidable \ce{NO_x} point to one policy. Monitor the stratospheric water and \ce{NO_x} burden as suborbital traffic grows, and gate expansion to the measured impact rather than capping flights in advance.

\section{Engineering Feasibility of the Near-Term PuffSat Architecture}\label{sec:engineering}

The near-term, low-orbit feasibility case rests on a preliminary closed-loop Monte Carlo simulation \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025}. It models the full path a PuffSat flies, from deployment near apogee (\SI{150000}{\kilo\meter}) down to interception near perigee (\SI{200}{\kilo\meter}). Under the modeled dispersions, it finds that capturing a \SI{5}{\meter} pusher plate is feasible. That result is conditional. It depends on the sensor and thruster grades fed in as inputs, assumes a conservative cannonball drag shape, and has not been cross-checked against an independent astrodynamics tool. It is a simulation result, not a flight result. What it changes is the navigation argument, which no longer leans only on analogy to GOCE.

The implementation offloads tracking and computation to three off-board navigation assets, none of them a dedicated co-flying coordinator satellite: an apogee navigation constellation that fixes the PuffSat's position through the GNSS-free coast, a target-side tracker array that drives terminal homing, and the reused launch rocket as a closer co-flying tracker, a hedge rather than a requirement. \autoref{sec:coordinator_node_dry_mass_disposal} develops these and explains why the low-orbit case needs no dedicated coordinator node.

The feasibility claim runs in three tiers, kept separate on purpose. Tier 1, capturing a \SI{5}{\meter} plate, is the closed-loop simulation result above. Tier 2, shrinking the plate toward \SI{10}{\centi\meter} with surveyor-anchored centering (a sacrificial lead PuffSat whose crossing is measured independently at the plate, calibrating out the tracker's fixed bias), is argued and sized but not simulated. Tier 3, carrying the same pattern to a near-Sun, Parker-class trajectory, is an architectural sketch with two open sizing numbers. Only Tier 1 is a simulation result; Tiers 2 and 3 are extrapolations.

\subsection{PuffSat Formation Challenges Compared To Current Missions}\label{sec:formation_challenges_current_missions}
Precise formation flying is rapidly advancing. The European Space Agency's (ESA) Proba-3 mission recently demonstrated millimeter-scale formation flying, creating artificial solar eclipses with a precise two-satellite formation \cite{llorente2013proba3,zhukov2025proba3aspiics}. The Stanford Space Rendezvous Laboratory's VISORS mission \cite{guffanti2023autonomous}, nearing flight readiness (as of June 2026), will demonstrate centimeter-precision formation flying with 6U CubeSats for \SI{10}{\second} durations at a \SI{600}{\kilo\meter} altitude \cite{visors_formation_flying}.

Our hypervelocity PuffSats employ formation-flying algorithms for precise positioning, but with a greater margin for error than VISORS or Proba-3. Those missions demanded millimeter-scale formation control and arcsecond-level pointing for imaging \cite{llorente2013proba3}, whereas PuffSats can have wider rotational misalignments and still satisfy mission objectives. Likewise, the lateral requirement is set by the size of the pusher plate, not by imaging-grade centering. Success means landing each PuffSat on the \SI{5}{\meter} plate, a tolerance near \SI{2}{\meter}. Reading the achievable centimeter-class centering as the requirement would conflate what the formation can do with what the mission needs. The centering the formation can actually achieve, with LED beacon metrology, is argued to be about \SI{10}{\centi\meter}, with \SI{5}{\centi\meter} only as a stretch. That capability is margin, well inside the \SI{2}{\meter} the plate allows. The cost of the looser tolerance falls on the reaction control system, which must cancel the off-center torque from a PuffSat that lands up to \SI{2}{\meter} from center. The plate's own rotational inertia softens that torque.

It helps to treat the whole train of PuffSats as a single block. Part of the position error is common-mode, the same for every PuffSat in the block, like a shared bias in the modeled drag or a deployer offset they all inherit. That error moves the block as one, and re-pointing the aim point removes it, a shift of at most a few kilometers that the launch geometry affords cheaply. If every PuffSat is off by the same kilometer, aiming the whole salvo a kilometer the other way lands them all on target. A sacrificial lead PuffSat sharpens this further. Sent a short way ahead of the salvo, it flies past the target, and its miss measures the block's residual offset, so the target makes a small move to take the rest head-on.

What remains is the per-unit scatter, the way the PuffSats differ from one another, and only that has to fall within the catch radius, the largest miss the terminal guidance can still correct. This split between common-mode and per-unit error is the single most important lever in the architecture. It is also why a fluctuating atmosphere and an uncertain drag coefficient are less of a threat than they first appear, since both act on the whole formation nearly identically and so are mostly common-mode. Pulsed optical beacons drive the per-unit scatter down. Each PuffSat strobes a known pattern, and centroiding its image against the star field fixes its angle to a small fraction of a pixel \cite{liebe2002startracker}. A formation held that tightly lands its PuffSats in a small cluster near the plate's center, so the off-center spread at impact stays low.

Operating at a lower interception altitude (\SI{200}{\kilo\meter}) introduces stronger and more variable drag perturbations than at VISORS' \SI{600}{\kilo\meter} orbit. VISORS maintains a near-circular sun-synchronous orbit and is therefore exposed to drag continuously, whereas PuffSats spend only minutes below VISORS' altitude. While PuffSats traverse low altitudes at higher speeds, their brief dwell time at these heights limits the accumulation of drag-induced drift. Timing PuffSat interceptions at night, in colder regions, or during periods of low solar activity can reduce the atmospheric drag PuffSats experience during their descent.

GOCE, a larger satellite, achieved full drag compensation at altitudes as low as \SI{229}{\kilo\meter} using a streamlined aerodynamic profile coupled with \SI{20}{\milli\newton} ion thrusters \cite{goce_dragfree, goce_229km}. Though electric propulsion for CubeSats is actively being developed \cite{busek_iodine}, adapting GOCE's ion propulsion system for a PuffSat remains unrealistic due to mass and power constraints. However, PuffSats could be elongated like GOCE to reduce drag. Torque could be mitigated by modest spin stabilization or GOCE-like fins, combined with cold gas or small chemical thrusters for fine control.

GOCE is an encouraging precedent rather than a proof. It showed that controlled flight deep in the atmosphere is real, but it held its altitude with ion thrusters and measured drag with a kilogram-class accelerometer, neither of which a PuffSat can carry. The case that a PuffSat can hold adequate precision instead rests on a preliminary closed-loop control simulation, which models the interception directly and finds it within reach of cold-gas thrusters \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025}. That simulation leans on no onboard precision accelerometer, because the terminal drag is computed ahead of time from a ground-measured drag prior and cancelled on a schedule. Based on the crude GOCE-derived modeling discussed in \autoref{sec:estimate_cold_gas}, PuffSats using cold gas thrusters would expend at most \SI{400}{\gram} of propellant during descent through denser atmospheric layers below \SI{600}{\kilo\meter}, even with pessimistic assumptions. Estimated peak thrust requirements of approximately \SI{400}{\milli\newton} appear sufficient to counteract drag forces below VISORS' operational altitude. This conservative ``back-of-the-napkin'' estimate provides a very rough upper bound, with propellant for low-altitude drag cancellation comprising less than 2\% of a \SI{25}{\kilo\gram} PuffSat's total mass.

\subsection{Fine-Control Thruster Options Without A Pressurized Tank}\label{sec:fine_control_thrusters}
Cold gas thrusters, like all PuffSat components, must minimize dry mass to reduce impact risk with the pusher plate (see \autoref{sec:coordinator_node_dry_mass_disposal}). A conventional cold gas storage tank would likely need to be embedded within the PuffSat to protect it from micrometeoroid damage, which increases both volume and drag. These tanks are also typically expensive to manufacture and pose some risks of hitting the pusher plate as shrapnel.

Two primary control objectives dictate the requirements for the replacement propulsion system. Compensating for aerodynamic drag and providing torque cancellation requires a throttleable thrust of approximately \SI{400}{\milli\newton}, sustained for several minutes. That thrust figure is the peak drag force derived from GOCE atmospheric modeling (see \autoref{sec:estimate_cold_gas}). This favors a continuous feed of liquid or generated gas. Brief attitude and divert corrections need fast, discrete impulses, which favor a bank of pre-measured solid charges. A single PuffSat can carry more than one of the forms below, matched to these jobs.

\subsubsection{Liquid-Fed Gas Generation}
The liquid feed makes gas on demand instead of storing it under pressure. Metered valves release small charges of a gas-generating liquid, which decomposes into warm gas just upstream of a lightweight bladder. The bladder acts as a brief low-pressure reservoir, not a liquid propellant tank, and feeds the fine-control thrusters with gram-scale gas charges. For example, a system might catalytically decompose a gram-scale charge of concentrated hydrogen peroxide mixed with a similar mass of water. The water quenches the hot decomposition products, so the bladder briefly holds moderately heated, pressurized steam and oxygen rather than gas too hot for its thin film. Small amounts of fuel may be added both to supply additional thermal energy and to react with harsh oxidizers in the exhaust stream. Corrosion and residue are challenges in microfluidics, but the system's very short, low-altitude operating times partially mitigate them. Green monopropellant can also feed a higher-thrust hybrid mode. Rather than generating gas for the bladder, it decomposes directly in a thruster chamber to give short, stronger bursts as thrust approaches the \SI{400}{\milli\newton} maximum. The flight-proven green monopropellants suit this burst mode better than the bladder mode. NASA's Green Propellant Infusion Mission flew the HAN-based ASCENT propellant, formerly AF-M315E \cite{spores2013gpim}, and ECAPS in Sweden flew the ADN-based LMP-103S earlier on PRISMA \cite{anflo2009prisma}. Both are reduced-toxicity hydrazine replacements tuned for high specific impulse, with adiabatic flame temperatures between \SI{1900}{\kelvin} and \SI{2200}{\kelvin} \cite{green_monoprop_review}. That heat fits short, high-thrust pulses but works against the thin polymer bladders and microfluidics. The bladder path therefore stays on hydrogen peroxide, the classic green monopropellant and still a flight option for small satellites \cite{cassese2023htp}. High-test peroxide delivers a lower specific impulse than the HAN and ADN propellants. But its catalytic decomposition runs far cooler and leaves only water and oxygen, matching the thin-skin, biocompatible design.

If the plumbing and support hardware for this microfluidic system weigh no more than \SI{50}{\gram}, the total dry mass would likely be lower than that of a standard \SI{400}{\gram} nitrogen tank \cite{meyer_copv}. That \SI{50}{\gram} target is not yet demonstrated as a complete flight system, but it is within reach of existing hardware. A commercial catalytic hydrogen peroxide thruster from Takasago Fluidic Systems already delivers \SI{0.2}{\newton} from a \SI{22.7}{\gram} thruster and an \SI{8.0}{\gram} valve, a combined \SI{31}{\gram} that leaves margin for tankage and control within the budget \cite{takasago_h2o2_thruster}. Terrestrial microfluidic chips already run the catalytic hydrogen peroxide decomposition this design needs, with the exothermic reaction warming the microchannel as it proceeds \cite{yang2024microchannel}. For scale, a complete flight-proven MEMS cold-gas module masses about \SI{220}{\gram}, including its tank and four microthrusters \cite{nasa_soa_propulsion}. 

The compact geometry also reduces drag and makes ejection ahead of pusher contact more straightforward (see \autoref{sec:coordinator_node_dry_mass_disposal}). As the thin polymer bladder skin fragments travel through the gas plume toward the pusher, they are decelerated and ablated, likely limiting impact damage to the pusher's ablative layers. The increased complexity of microfluidics introduces the risk of catastrophic PuffSat failures. However, the system must already be resilient to PuffSat losses, given many are used on each launch.

\subsubsection{Cool Gas Generators}
Propellant can also be stored as a solid that makes gas on command. Cool gas generators (CGGs) decompose a solid charge into gases, then route them through a heat-absorbing filter so they leave cool. The charge sits unpressurized until fired, which removes both the launch hazard and the fragmentation risk of a pre-pressurized tank. This approach has nanosatellite flight heritage. The T$^3\mu$PS cold-gas system built around CGGs flew on TU Delft's Delfi-n3Xt \cite{cgg_delfi_n3xt}, where two generators were ignited in orbit before an ignition-train fault ended the test \cite{delfi_n3xt_inorbit}. It drew on earlier ESA cold gas generator work \cite{esa_cold_gas_generators}. For a PuffSat, the chemistry should be chosen so the exhaust stays biocompatible. Non-azide automotive gas generants fit well. Guanidine nitrate burned with a basic copper nitrate oxidizer produces mostly nitrogen, water, and carbon dioxide \cite{guanidine_nitrate_gas_generant}, the same biocompatible products this design favors elsewhere. These generants are mass-produced for automotive airbags. Roughly 93 million vehicles are built worldwide each year \cite{oica_production_2023}, almost all carrying multiple inflators. Annual output runs into the hundreds of millions of units, suiting our reliance on mass production. A phase-stabilized ammonium nitrate formulation is also attractive, since it shares chemistry with the ANFO PuffSat fill (see \autoref{sec:explosive_puffsat}) and burns by controlled deflagration rather than detonation. Two chemistries should be avoided. Classic sodium-azide inflators leave a solid sodium-silicate slag, which is the non-volatile shrapnel we work to eliminate. Aluminized solid-rocket grains leave alumina slag and a metal casing for the same reason. Even clean generants emit trace carbon monoxide and nitrogen oxides and burn hotter than a cold-gas blowdown, so the heat-absorbing filter stays necessary to protect the thin skin and microfluidics.

\subsubsection{Single-Shot Impulse Generators}
Firing many small, individually triggered charges for the divert job has a clear precedent in missile defense. The Patriot PAC-3 interceptor steers the same way for a kinetic, hit-to-kill strike. In its terminal phase it fires individual solid attitude control motors from a ring of 180 in the forebody, each a single shot, to drive its own body onto the target \cite{pac3_acm}. A PuffSat could carry a similar set of single-shot gas-generator pulses, each producing clean gas rather than the metal-loaded exhaust of the Patriot motors.

Because PuffSats are single-use and operate for only half of a single orbit, we can budget more propellant for aggressive drag and torque correction. For PuffSats, overestimating propellant needs is wise. Unused gas is not entirely wasted, since its momentum still lands on the pusher plate rather than venting into deep space. Variability in this leftover gas propulsion slightly complicates the target rocket's trajectory adjustments between impacts. Each impact also lands a little off the plate's center, so the rocket must cancel the resulting torque. Its reaction-control thrusters and a gimbaled main engine that can tilt to push off-axis handle this, both running non-toxic propellant. We expect the rocket to trim its trajectory after each pulse as well, correcting for the gap between a pulse's true impulse and the value it was planned against. Our control simulation covered the PuffSat side of the interception, not the rocket's, so this rocket-side response is a requirement we flag rather than a simulated result. The first few PuffSats to intercept the target rocket will experience the lowest altitudes (closest to \SI{200}{\kilo\meter}) before the rocket rises slightly as it accelerates towards orbit (see \autoref{sec:leo_orbit_details}). To optimize propellant usage, we can equip these initial units with slightly more propellant to compensate for their more challenging trajectory.

If control remains impractical, we can simplify by increasing the PuffSat interception altitude. If we raise interception to \SI{300}{\kilo\meter}, a reusable suborbital target rocket (see \autoref{sec:200_mile_high}) would need higher propellant mass fractions, suffer more re-entry heating, and gain less from PuffSat velocity. However, raising interception height may be an acceptable compromise if lower altitudes near \SI{200}{\kilo\meter} prove unmanageable.

\subsection{Coordinator Node Architecture And Dry Mass Disposal}\label{sec:coordinator_node_dry_mass_disposal}
To maximize gas mass, the PuffSats carry only simple, low-mass electronics and offload tracking and computation to off-board nodes. One option is a small number of coordinator satellites with enhanced computing power. They fly alongside the formation, and each tracks the PuffSats' positions and relays adjustment commands, accounting for communication and actuator latencies in its control trajectories. As shown in \autoref{fig:coordinator-nodes}, these heavier nodes do not impact the target rocket. The closed-loop control simulation, though, finds that a dedicated coordinator satellite is not required for the low-orbit case \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025}. The tracking-and-relay role can split across three assets already in the architecture. During the multi-day coast there is no GNSS, so position knowledge comes from a Ka-band authenticated ranging constellation parked near apogee, roughly \SI{150000}{\kilo\meter} out. For terminal homing, a target-side tracker array images each PuffSat's beacon against the star field and drives the final correction. The reused launch rocket adds a closer co-flying tracker as a hedge. The coordinator satellite stays available where extra on-board computing or relay is wanted, but the baseline no longer depends on it. Keeping the PuffSats this simple also suits mass production, since hundreds are needed per mission.

Inspired by the smallest PocketQube pico-satellite designs \cite{pocketqube_mass_reference}, each PuffSat carries no more than \SI{250}{\gram} of dry mass. This dry mass includes sensors, power systems, microthrusters, and low-bandwidth communication links to the coordinator nodes. If typical PuffSats carry more than \SI{25}{\kilo\gram} of fuel and propulsion mass, non-volatile solids constitute less than 1\% of their total mass. To prevent this dry mass from impacting the rocket, we propose several disposal strategies:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Discard Before Impact:} The dry mass components could launch slightly away from the rocket just before impact. If explosives are used to gasify the PuffSat (see \autoref{sec:explosive_puffsat}), shaped explosive sections can direct and accelerate the non-volatile solids radially outward at high speed. The shaped explosives ensure the non-volatile solids outrun the expanding gas envelope and clear the target radius prior to impact. The shaped explosives also partially decelerate the solids, lowering their perigee to further reduce the risk of creating space debris (see \autoref{sec:handling_space_debris}). \label{item:discard_before_impact}
    \item \textbf{Active Elimination:} Equipment on the target rocket could obliterate the dry mass via electric discharges, directed energy beams, or sprays of material that hit the incoming PuffSat solids before pusher plate impact.
    \item \textbf{Passive Routing:} The dry mass components could pass harmlessly through a small aperture in the pusher plate.
\end{itemize} 

All of these impact mitigation techniques would occasionally fail. In this case, the pusher plate itself is a last line of defense. A heat-tolerant Kevlar-backed pusher plate (see \autoref{sec:lightweight_pusher_plates}) structurally resembles Whipple shielding \cite{whipple_shield}. The pusher plate would pulverize the non-volatile solids and prevent catastrophic damage to crewed compartments or high-value satellites. The target rocket would then ideally steer out of the PuffSat stream and abort. If it carries people, it survives reentry with single-use but reliable ablative heat shields. The vehicle might also carry swappable, redundant pusher plates to allow mission continuation after a single component failure. Even with redundancy, we retain the capability for reentry in a worst-case multiple-failure scenario.

\subsubsection{Steering PuffSats With High-Powered Lasers}
Instead of relying on non-volatile solid components, radiation pressure could directly manipulate PuffSats. Coordinator nodes might employ kilowatt-class lasers to adjust PuffSat trajectories. The intense laser light could also induce temporary thermal or photochromic changes in the PuffSat's skin, altering its geometry or albedo. If these changes briefly persist, the altered solar radiation pressure would magnify PuffSat trajectory changes compared to the laser's radiation pressure alone. Eliminating non-volatile solid components on PuffSats reduces the risk of pusher plate damage in an accident. The PuffSats cost less without microthrusters and electronics. However, space lasers present drawbacks compared to microthrusters. They exert weak forces on the PuffSats and require kilowatt-class power supplies on coordinator nodes. 

\subsection{A Rough Sketch Of The Software Guidance For PuffSat Interception}\label{sec:neural_navigation}
To ensure a precise interception of the next PuffSat, the target spacecraft's rocket-based reaction control system must rapidly adjust its trajectory. It must do this within fractions of a second after each impact, correcting for the difference between the expected and actual momentum transfer from the previous PuffSat. A prerequisite for these hypervelocity PuffSat rendezvous is accurately capturing the position and velocity of the PuffSats. For this, a standard Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF) \cite{wan2000unscented} offers robust state estimation when dynamics are well-modeled and noise is Gaussian. The large formation of PuffSats provides a dense measurement stream, further improving filter performance. 

PuffSat interceptions, however, may occur near low periapsis in denser atmospheric layers where drag, gravitational anomalies, sensor dropouts, and outliers challenge UKF's assumptions and accuracy. When these effects exceed UKF tolerance, state estimation could be improved with hybrid approaches that augment the UKF with neural networks that learn residuals on real and simulated orbital data. For example, methods like KalmanNet \cite{revach_2022_kalmannet,revach2022unsupervised} have shown robustness to unmodeled disturbances in simulations. Neural inference also scales better as the number of estimated states grows; the UKF's covariance update costs roughly the cube of the state dimension, while a trained network's forward pass grows far more slowly. As a result, neural estimators may support more frequent updates in a large PuffSat formation. 

Interpretable, data-efficient techniques like model predictive control (MPC) can replan trajectories to correct for momentum prediction errors after PuffSat impacts. Neural networks can warm-start conventional tools with good initial guesses to reduce solve times \cite{guffanti2024transformerstrajectoryoptimizationapplication}, especially when there are many real-time constraints \cite{briden_constraint}. However, the control law itself could remain grounded in well-understood optimization and stability guarantees. 

Since conservative approaches appear feasible, we've prioritized them over faster, more opaque methods, such as parallel deep learning ensembles optimized for inference speed. That said, these black-box strategies merit consideration. Our PuffSat system must already tolerate frequent hardware failures such as lost PuffSats, so an occasional control error from a black-box method is no worse than the anomalies we already plan for. Reinforcement learning even shows promise for developing fast trajectory recovery algorithms after such anomalies, where standard control techniques struggle \cite{zavoli2021reinforcement}.

The binding lateral requirement is only to land on the \SI{5}{\meter} plate, a tolerance near \SI{2}{\meter}; tighter centering is a refinement, not a prerequisite. Gas generation unfolds over a finite duration and has less precision along the thrust axis. Centering the impact to roughly \SI{10}{\centi\meter}, which the beacon metrology is argued to reach, makes the plume impinge symmetrically and preserves the vehicle's inertial stability. The target rocket's reaction control system must still cancel the off-center torque from the occasional PuffSat that lands well off center, the price of the relaxed lateral tolerance.

The terminal endgame is forgiving on control authority. The PuffSat's thrusters can pull it back onto the plate from a miss of roughly \SI{475}{\meter} \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025}, the catch radius the block argument above relies on. That distance is fixed by how far constant thrust moves a mass in the time remaining, the familiar $\tfrac{1}{2}a t^2$, not by how well the PuffSat is tracked.

As the PuffSat closes on the plate, the time remaining shrinks and the reachable sideways distance shrinks with it, so the correctable region is a funnel that narrows toward the plate. The closer the PuffSat gets, the less room it has to swerve, and the size of that last swerve is set by its thrusters, not its eyesight. Because the recoverable miss runs far larger than the few-meter plate, the PuffSat enters this funnel with roughly a factor of two to spare on authority. What sets the real limit is therefore not control but knowledge, how well the PuffSat knows its sideways position in the final seconds.

That knowledge is the binding constraint, and it takes a simple form. The cross-track error is the tracker's angular precision $\sigma_\theta$ times the range $R$ to the PuffSat, so $\sigma_\theta R$ is the quantity that must be held small. The simulation puts the requirement at an effective angular precision of about \SI{3}{\micro\radian} on the target tracker \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025}. What makes this hard is that the floor is a fixed calibration bias in the optics, not random noise.

That distinction reorders the priorities. The problem is not a shaky image that steadies if you take more exposures. It is closer to a ruler with a slightly bent end, and averaging more readings cannot straighten a bent ruler. The obvious control fixes therefore do nothing, because more thrust or a later burn cannot buy knowledge the tracker never had. The obvious sensor fixes do nothing either, because longer integration and heavier filtering attack noise, not a bias. What works is geometric: sharpen the angle by calibrating the optics or differencing against known stars, or shorten the range $R$ by tracking from closer in.

The terminal tracker is built to pull both levers. Its effective grade of about \SI{3}{\micro\radian} comes not from one exquisite sensor but from fusing several independent detectors on the target, optionally helped by a co-flying rocket much closer to the action. Each detector makes its own star-referenced measurement through its own separately calibrated optics, so their distortion biases are largely independent. Their combined bias falls as $1/\sqrt{N}$, so about a dozen detectors at \SI{10}{\micro\radian} each average down to the \SI{3}{\micro\radian} grade \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025}. This is the cross-detector averaging a single optic cannot do against its own fixed bias.

The co-flyer pulls on the other handle in $\sigma_\theta R$. Parked close to the converging PuffSats, even a modest angular precision gives it a small sideways error, because $R$ is small. Casting terminal navigation as a fused, redundant array makes it both more accurate and safer, since the redundancy protects the result if any one optic's calibration proves worse than hoped.

\subsection{Handling Space Debris} \label{sec:handling_space_debris}
The dry mass components of each PuffSat risk becoming space debris. To avoid this, we can intercept the PuffSats prior to their orbital periapsis. For example, a PuffSat interception at a \SI{200}{\kilo\meter} altitude likely follows a predictable trajectory. If the PuffSat periapsis is near \SI{50}{\kilo\meter}, this deorbits any dry mass components. We time them to burn up or fall over remote oceans. PuffSats that miss their target rockets either boil or explode from reentry heating, eliminating the risk of heavy debris reaching the ground. Two other techniques prevent long-lasting space debris:

\begin{itemize}
\item The $J_2$ perturbation makes a satellite's argument of periapsis drift over time \cite{brouwer1959satellite}, except at a few critical inclinations where it stays fixed. By choosing an inclination away from those values, we let the periapsis slowly rotate until it lies over the equator. Since the Earth bulges at the equator \cite{nima_wgs84}, the periapsis then sits at a lower altitude with much more drag, which deorbits the dry mass.
\item If our PuffSat interceptions occur at night, subsequent daytime periapsis will be over a warmer atmosphere with higher drag at the same altitude.
\end{itemize}

The low perigee, near \SIrange{50}{65}{\kilo\meter}, is a designed disposal outcome rather than a targeting error. It deorbits each spent PuffSat and burns up any unit that misses its target, so a lower perigee is safer. A reader should take \SI{50}{\kilo\meter} as a disposal margin, not a navigation fault to correct upward. A preliminary closed-loop simulation of the interception supports this clean-disposal picture. Its full guidance budget, cruise corrections plus the terminal aim, holds under 2\% of a \SI{25}{\kilo\gram} PuffSat's mass even in the worst sampled case \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025}. A budget that small needs no large propellant tank, so the guidance system adds little dry mass to dispose of.

An alternative removes the down-kick at its source rather than cancelling it at the plate, and it pays off only when the fudge factor $f$ is low. Raising the PuffSat perigee to the \SI{200}{\kilo\meter} interception altitude catches each PuffSat at its perigee, with no descending velocity. There is then no downward momentum for the plate to handle by angling (see \autoref{sec:lightweight_pusher_plates}). That plate cancellation is cheap when $f$ is high and the rebound is elastic. A low $f$ leaves a large residual down-force, and that is when paying to remove the down-kick instead becomes worthwhile. The catch is disposal. A \SI{200}{\kilo\meter} perigee does not self-deorbit, so each dry-mass package fires a small burn at its next apogee, dropping its perigee to about \SI{50}{\kilo\meter} for reentry. Because the apogee of a near-escape orbit is far out, where the package is barely moving, that burn is only about \SI{4}{\meter\per\second}. That is well within the microthrusters the dry mass already carries, and far below the roughly \SI{1.4}{\kilo\meter\per\second} the same change would cost down at perigee. The package coasts about one extra orbit, a day or so, before it deorbits. The hardware must be highly reliable, because a dud strands the package on the \SI{200}{\kilo\meter} orbit as long-lived debris.

Since PuffSats form large formations, care must also be taken to avoid existing satellites. Short, seconds-long gaps in the formation could be intentionally introduced to avoid collisions.

\subsection{PuffSat Deployment Details For Low Earth Orbit Scenario}\label{sec:leo_orbit_details}
Let's consider the complexities of launching target rockets into low Earth orbit with propulsion from PuffSats deployed in highly eccentric Earth orbits. A purely low, circular orbit is not an option for them. Atmospheric drag at the roughly \SI{200}{\kilo\meter} interception altitude would decay such an orbit within days. Each PuffSat instead rides a highly eccentric ellipse that only dips briefly through the dense low-altitude band, with a \SI{50}{\kilo\meter} perigee that disposes of any miss (see \autoref{sec:handling_space_debris}).

These PuffSats will not follow strictly identical Keplerian orbits. As the target rocket accelerates, the optimal PuffSat interception point moves along with it, so that successive PuffSats have slightly different orbital elements.
For low-mass PuffSats, solar radiation pressure will distort their orbits. To simplify orbit planning and maintain consistent effects across the fleet, it's ideal for each PuffSat to remain outside the shadow of the others. This may necessitate slight variations in orbital parameters, such as inclination, for nearby PuffSats. The microthrusters on each PuffSat make corrections for unmodeled perturbations. We still ensure each PuffSat achieves the correct interception position with the target rocket. 

The eccentric orbit carries each PuffSat through the Van Allen radiation belts \cite{li2019vanallen} on its inbound leg, so the electronics face only a single pass of dose, not a repeated orbit. The PuffSat's own propellant mass shields the avionics buried inside it. The outer belt is dominated by energetic electrons, which modest material stops. The inner belt holds high-energy protons that are harder to shield, but one fast transit keeps the accumulated dose low enough to accept. Where more margin is needed, triple-modular redundancy, running three copies of the electronics and taking a majority vote, tolerates occasional radiation upsets with commercial-grade parts rather than fully radiation-hardened ones. SpaceX's Dragon flies this way and rode out the loss of one of its three flight computers to a radiation hit \cite{aviationweek2012dragon}.

Deploying PuffSats near apoapsis minimizes the $\Delta V$ required for both inclination changes and optimal satellite spacing. This reduction in required thrust suggests that mechanical launchers with moderate size, power, and peak acceleration could deploy PuffSats very precisely with sufficient velocity changes. The launch mechanism could use rotating wheels or computer-controlled rail catapults, with recoil forces countered by the rocket's reaction control systems. PuffSats can be launched in series, further reducing launch mechanism operational demands.

\subsection{Lightweight Pusher Plate Design: Materials and Damping Innovations Beyond Orion}\label{sec:lightweight_pusher_plates}
Modern materials allow pusher plates far lighter than those Project Orion contemplated. Although individual PuffSat pulses may reach peak shock pressures comparable to Orion's nuclear impacts, their total impulse is orders of magnitude lower. This lower total impulse permits thinner structural elements, just as rifle barrels withstand high pressures with far less mass than \SI{155}{\milli\meter} artillery barrels \cite{cip_308win, ohara1999_155mm_breech}. Unlike Orion, PuffSat plates don't need to shield crew from nuclear radiation. The absence of bomb radiation also extends longevity for pusher plate materials like complex aramid polymers.

Kevlar liners, unavailable during Orion's development, offer lightweight spall protection against impact fragmentation \cite{aramid_ballistic_review}. Titanium alloys, used in artillery that operates under repeated firing shocks such as the M777 Howitzer \cite{titanium_m777}, are viable candidates for structural plate substrates. Fracture- and corrosion-resistant silicon nitride liners, fabricated as in-situ-rifled gun barrel liners for high-temperature erosion service \cite{si3n4_gun_liner}, could serve as a protective interface in PuffSat pushers. Silicon nitride liners mitigate chemical corrosion across different PuffSat gas chemistries, including pure, superheated oxygen (see \autoref{sec:lox_puffsat}). Temperature-resistant superalloys like Inconel 718 \cite{inconel_718_strong} can supplement or replace titanium where heat or cost justify the additional weight penalty.

An alternative architecture draws inspiration from Project Medusa \cite{solem_medusa}, a conceptual successor to Orion. Medusa replaced Orion's rigid plates with flexible, parachute-like tensile sails. Scaled-down, high-tensile fabric sheets could absorb PuffSat impulses with reduced mass and simplified deployment above the atmosphere. Since PuffSats approach from the rear, these flexible pushers would be mounted behind the rocket on shock-absorbing struts. One candidate for the struts might be thermally coated, reinforced carbon fiber with titanium or superalloy joints. Carbon fiber offers high strength-to-weight and thermal stability. The metal alloy joints prevent fracture at peak shock points.

A sail and a rigid plate sit at two ends of one tradeoff. The sail absorbs each pulse over a long stroke with a light membrane, while a rigid plate takes the same pulse over a short stroke and carries more mass. Either can be shaped concave on its gas-facing side to wrap the divergent puff and collimate the rebound forward, the same curvature that raises the effective $f$ (see \autoref{sec:mass_fraction}).

Mounting the sail behind the rocket carries a structural cost. Real Medusa flew its sail ahead of the payload with the blast in the gap between them, so every connecting member was in tension and could be a long, light cable \cite{solem_medusa}. Our sail rides behind the rocket, where the gas must overtake the craft and strike the sail's rear face. So those members are in compression instead, and compression members buckle. The Euler buckling load falls as the square of the member's length, which makes a long compression strut a far harder problem than a long tether. The penalty is paid down, not removed. A compression boom stays buildable if it is braced like a sailboat mast by guy wires in tension, and telescoped so its peak load falls at its shortest length.

Keeping the sail behind is a deliberate fail-safe. Adding forward momentum with external reaction mass forces each PuffSat to approach from the rear and overtake the craft. A front-mounted tension sail, the true-Medusa arrangement, would route every hypervelocity PuffSat past the crew cabin. A rear sail shields the crew instead. If its silhouette is at least as large as the craft, any PuffSat that misses the sail also clears the craft, and overshoots or duds fly off into empty space behind. The compression and buckling penalty is the price of keeping people out of the firing line. Uncrewed cargo can instead use a front-mounted tension sail, with its light cables and no buckling, accepting that a rare strike costs a vehicle rather than lives. A front sail split symmetrically to the sides narrows that gap. Built paired or annular so the thrust stays on axis, it routes PuffSats up the flanks and keeps even a crewed cabin off the centerline. That layout is only partially fail-safe, since a mis-aimed solid can still clip a flank rather than flying off behind, and it costs harder off-axis aiming.

In contrast to Medusa's massive nuclear parachutes, PuffSat-compatible sheets can be thicker yet remain flexible, tailored to smaller impulse magnitudes. A practical implementation weaves the sail itself from continuous ceramic-fiber cloth. Suitable yarns include 3M Nextel 720 alumina--mullite \cite{nextel_720_fiber} and Hi-Nicalon Type S silicon carbide. Both retain useful tensile strength and remain drapeable above \SI{1200}{\celsius} \cite{nextel_720_hot_tensile, hi_nicalon_sic}.

The woven fabric is then shielded from PuffSat exhaust by a nanometer-scale environmental barrier coating deposited by CVD or atomic-layer deposition \cite{ebc_review}. Silicon carbide or silicon nitride layers \cite{silicon_ceramic_oxidation} form a self-healing silica scale in oxygen-rich plasma. Hexagonal boron nitride is the most compliant interphase available for ceramic composites \cite{uhtcmc_review}. However, it volatilizes as B\textsubscript{2}O\textsubscript{3} above $\sim$\SI{1200}{\celsius} in oxidizing atmospheres \cite{bn_oxidation}, so it is reserved for inert-gas PuffSat chemistries. A compliant high-temperature silicone overlay \cite{silicon_coatings} can serve as a secondary sealant on cooler regions of the sail.

The coated cloth is only the outer ply. The whole sail can be built as a stitched laminate that splits the thermal and structural work between materials, much as NASA's Hypersonic Inflatable Aerodynamic Decelerator builds its flexible heat shield \cite{hiad_flexible_tps}. That design lays a high-temperature reinforcing fabric of Nextel or Nicalon yarn over one or more layers of porous insulation, such as Pyrogel or Saffil felt, backed by a thin inner gas barrier, with the plies stitched together rather than rigidly bonded. A PuffSat sail can adopt the same stack. The coated ceramic cloth is the plasma-facing ply. A ceramic felt batting sits behind it. The innermost ply is a polymer tensile net that carries the integrated impulse load back to the struts and adds the fold endurance and shock toughness that brittle ceramic yarn lacks. Vectran, a liquid-crystal polymer fiber, suits this role. It holds tension without creep, so the sail stays taut across many pulses, and it tolerates higher temperatures than polyethylene \cite{vectran_brochure}. Stitching the plies, rather than bonding them face to face, also avoids delamination from the large mismatch in thermal expansion between ceramic and polymer. A brief, widely spaced pulse cannot drive heat through the felt before radiation sheds it, so the back ply runs far cooler than the incandescent face. The polymer sets the temperature ceiling, and the candidates form a ladder from Dyneema through Vectran and aramid to coated PBO, which holds usefully high and decomposes only near \SI{650}{\celsius} \cite{zylon_technical_info} (see \autoref{sec:fiber_selection}). The felt must hold the back ply below the chosen fiber's limit across the whole pulse train. The sail's radiative equilibrium temperature, fixed by pulse energy, areal density, emissivity, and pulse spacing, decides how far up that ladder the net must climb. Each step toward PBO trades away shock toughness, so the net climbs no higher than the heat forces.

This layered sail differs from a reentry heat shield in the way that drives the material choice. Starship seals the gaps between its silica tiles with a wrapped felt gasket, the ``crunch wrap,'' replacing the loose gap fillers carried over from the Shuttle \cite{starship_crunchwrap}. Those tiles, and the bare stainless steel of the leeward side, only have to insulate and resist oxidation. The steel hull behind them stays cool and carries every structural load, so the tiles themselves bear almost no tension \cite{starship}. A Medusa sail has no cool rigid hull in the hot zone. Its plasma-facing ply must hold membrane tension while incandescent, carrying each pulse across the short unsupported spans between stitch lines and into the net below. That is why the hot face needs Nextel 720 or Hi-Nicalon Type S, yarns that keep tensile strength above \SI{1200}{\celsius} \cite{nextel_720_hot_tensile, hi_nicalon_sic}, not a silica tile chosen only to insulate.

The sail's organic parts must survive the orbital environment as well as the plasma. The carbon-fiber struts and the polymer net both face atomic oxygen and ultraviolet in low orbit \cite{ao_protection_banks}, so both carry the same metallized or silica barrier described in \autoref{sec:fiber_selection}. That one low-outgassing layer reflects the ultraviolet and seals out the atomic oxygen, and behind it the vacuum keeps the fiber dry.

The metallized net carries the self-healing overlay that \autoref{sec:fiber_selection} describes, since micrometeoroid and thermal-cycling defects would otherwise let atomic oxygen undercut the fiber. The sail's ceramic plies need no overlay. They already reglaze themselves with a silica scale in oxygen-rich plasma, and their oxide and silicon-carbide chemistries resist atomic oxygen and ultraviolet without outgassing. The carbon-fiber struts still need a space-qualified resin so the matrix does not shed volatiles in vacuum.

Project Orion proposed that sacrificial ablative oils pre-applied to the pusher's skin would fully protect it from plasma vaporization \cite{projorion}. However, PuffSat gases like pure oxygen, carbon dioxide and water contain oxygen and will decompose at impact temperatures. This oxygen would aggressively attack flammable phenolic ablators like PICA \cite{pica_ablator_milos2010}. Adding flame retardants and materials like boron carbide and hafnium carbide increases the oxygen resistance of phenolic resins like PICA \cite{phenolic_oxidation_hfc_b4c}.

The extreme conditions may still make the non-flammable ``solar salts'' used for solar storage superior ablators \cite{hitec_solar_salt}. Solar salts can absorb large amounts of thermal energy and decompose at temperatures far below the thermal failure point for advanced metal alloys or ceramic composites. Solar salts become oxidizers when they decompose \cite{nitrate_salt_decomposition}. The Nextel 720 / Hi-Nicalon woven sail with its environmental barrier coating should withstand this corrosion, as should the silicon nitride liners we discussed. Whether we choose brittle solar salts or PICA-based ablators, they can both be embedded as microparticles in single-use coatings applied to the woven ceramic sail so they stick to deformable Medusa-style pushers. 

Without nuclear bombs subjecting semiconductors to extreme radiation, the target vehicle can use large parallel arrays of space-qualified inkjet-style MEMS to evenly deposit micron-thick layers of ablative liquids across the pusher plate. The ablative materials could be silicone-based, organic oils, or ionic liquids. In a vacuum, each droplet follows a predictable ballistic path, eliminating the need for bulky robotic sprayers. This path must account for transient accelerations imparted by the shock absorber system, as well as any impulse vectors from reaction control system firings, but remains precisely calculable in vacuum. If the sprayed ablative is only 1 or 2 microns thick, the total mass is likely \SI{100}{\gram} or less for a \SI{25}{\square\meter} plate. These coatings can be formulated to vaporize under peak impulse heating, sacrificing themselves to protect the permanent pusher plate structure.

As opposed to Orion, our propulsion system includes reaction control thrusters (see \autoref{sec:neural_navigation}). These can be positioned forward on the target vehicle, increasing standoff distance and minimizing thermal or directional interference with the pusher plate. 

Shock absorber technology has advanced since Orion. Magnetorheological (MR) fluids offer dynamic vibration damping, reducing the risk of carbon-fiber strut fracture under repeated loading \cite{mr_fluids}. To further reduce mass and thermal buildup, magnetic braking systems can complement mechanical springs and hydraulics. Rare-earth magnets \cite{rare_earth_braking} or high-field REBCO superconducting tapes \cite{rebco_tapes} enable compact magnetic dampers that earlier magnet materials were too weak to support. REBCO tapes are, however, still an emerging technology and require cryogenic refrigeration and superconductor quench management. Because PuffSat impulses are scalable, we can deliberately reduce acceleration per pulse, further relaxing shock absorber requirements.

Unlike Orion, the PuffSat plate has no critical mass. Orion's plate was forced large by the kiloton floor on nuclear yield, because no bomb can be made arbitrarily small \cite{balcomb1970nuclear}. PuffSat pulse energy scales down smoothly, so the plate can shrink with the pulse. The buffer a plate must supply, its mass times its stroke, falls steeply as the pulse rate rises, roughly as its inverse square. That is one dividend, not two. Faster, smaller pulses buy either a lighter plate at the same stroke or a shorter stroke at the same mass, never both at once. At a pulse rate of a few hertz, a stroke of a few meters holds the plate near a tenth of the craft mass, a few tonnes on the \SI{32}{\tonne} vehicle of our low-orbit case. A floor still waits underneath. Survival sets a minimum areal density, the plate's mass per unit area, of order tens of kilograms per square meter. That is far below Orion's regime, and below what the buffer already demands. So here the dynamics, not survival, set the plate near a tenth of the craft.

A higher pulse rate brings a cost of its own. It drives the ripple, the leftover swing in the craft's acceleration between pulses, up into the \SIrange{4}{8}{\hertz} band where the body is most sensitive to vertical vibration \cite{iso2631}. There a single-stage absorber would let the craft swing by several g on every pulse. A two-stage absorber, carried over from Orion, fixes this \cite{projorion}. A stiff, short-stroke first stage catches the per-pulse blow, and a soft second stage tuned well below the pulse rate isolates the crew. Tuned that low, the second stage cuts the ripple by more than an order of magnitude, leaving a nearly steady push.

Catching PuffSats on the descending leg also adds a downward push that the plate's orientation can cancel. Because PuffSats are intercepted while still falling toward their deliberately low \SI{50}{\kilo\meter} disposal perigee (see \autoref{sec:handling_space_debris}), the momentum each one delivers is not purely forward. It carries a downward component of about $\sin\gamma$ of the impulse, where $\gamma$ is the PuffSat's flight-path angle below the craft's velocity. At the \SI{200}{\kilo\meter} interception altitude this orbit gives $\gamma \approx \SI{8.5}{\degree}$, so $\sin\gamma$ is about 15\%. That 15\% is a floor. The downward part of each pulse holds near \SI{1.6}{\kilo\meter\per\second}, while the overtaking velocity that drives the forward thrust shrinks as the craft speeds up, so the downward fraction climbs through the boost and approaches roughly one-half as the craft nears orbital speed (\autoref{sec:down_angle_geometry}). The component has the same sign on every pulse, so it accumulates and bends the trajectory Earthward unless the plate cancels it.

The cancellation is largely geometric, and cheap rather than free. What sets the net force on the craft is the plate's orientation relative to the thrust axis (the craft's forward direction of flight), not its orientation relative to the line back to the PuffSat. A plate held normal to the thrust axis reverses the gas's forward component and lets the downward component pass straight back out. The rebound then carries most of the unwanted downward momentum away, and the craft feels nearly pure forward thrust. A plate aimed square at the incoming PuffSat would instead double the downward kick. The plate is opaque, so little of the gas escapes sideways past it. Opacity is also what keeps the impact near-elastic and $f$ high, so the clean reflection and the high-$f$ design rest on the same property. A preliminary single-code hydrodynamic analysis supports the low spill \cite{Katz_puffsat_impact_sim_2026}. Its loss decomposition puts sideways escape below the condensation and conduction channels across the insertion band, including the cool \SI{3.2}{\kilo\meter\per\second} worst case. The downward component therefore reflects cleanly instead of leaking past the plate edge. The pass-through is exact only in the elastic limit, though. At the working $f \approx 0.8$ the bounce is partly inelastic, so the plate retains a fraction of the downward momentum rather than returning all of it, leaving a residual downward force. Because the downward fraction climbs toward one-half late in the boost, this residual is largest near orbital speed. The active trim below, or the reaction-control thrusters, removes it at a small cost in forward thrust.

Neutralizing the kick this way is usually enough. If a steady upward trim $F_{\mathrm{up}}$ is wanted on top of it, the plate is tilted slightly further off the thrust axis so the reflected gas is thrown a little downward. The craft then feels an upward reaction set by the full rebounded momentum rather than a small spill fraction. Such a trim, applied at the rear plate a distance $d$ behind the center of mass, would pitch the craft. Placing the impact point a height $h = d\,(F_{\mathrm{up}}/F_{\mathrm{fwd}})$ below the centerline makes the forward thrust's couple cancel that pitch. The resultant then passes through the center of mass, and the trim costs nothing in attitude. This shaped plate inherits its momentum coupling from Orion's pusher plate \cite{projorion} and its concave, expansion-wrapping geometry from Medusa's sail \cite{solem_medusa}. The curvature that turns the rebound forward to raise $f$ (see \autoref{sec:mass_fraction}) is the same curvature that lets the rebound carry the downward momentum out, so the down-push fix and the high-$f$ design are one plate-shape decision with two payoffs.

\section{PuffSat cold-gas propellant: pessimistic upper-bound estimate}\label{sec:estimate_cold_gas}

We scale GOCE to a geometrically similar \(\SI{25}{\kg}\) PuffSat composed of low-density ANFO (see \autoref{sec:explosive_puffsat}). This gives a rough, intentionally pessimistic upper bound for cold-gas propellant needs.  We model GOCE as a cylinder and assume to first order that drag scales with surface area.  Our conservative multiplicative assumptions follow.

\subsection*{Geometry and geometric scaling}
Model GOCE as a cylinder with diameter \(D_0=\SI{1.0}{\m}\) and length \(L_0=\SI{4.0}{\meter}\).  Then the radius \(r_0=\tfrac{D_0}{2}=\SI{0.5}{\meter}\), and
\[
V_0 = \pi r_0^2 L_0 = \pi\cdot(0.5)^2\cdot 4 = \pi\ \mathrm{m^3},
\qquad
S_0 = 2\pi r_0 L_0 + 2\pi r_0^2 = \tfrac{9}{2}\pi\ \mathrm{m^2}.
\]

Let the PuffSat mass be \(m=\SI{25}{\kg}\) and assume an ANFO density
\(\rho=\SI{840}{\kg\per\m^3}\).  The PuffSat volume is
\[
V=\frac{m}{\rho}.
\]

If the PuffSat is geometrically similar to GOCE, the linear scale factor \(s\) satisfies
\[
V = s^3 V_0
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
s = \left(\frac{V}{V_0}\right)^{1/3}.
\]
Surface area scales as \(S = s^2 S_0\), so the area ratio is
\[
\frac{S}{S_0} = s^2 = \left(\frac{m/\rho}{V_0}\right)^{2/3}.
\]

\paragraph{Numeric evaluation (ANFO, \(\rho=\SI{840}{\kg\per\m^3}\)).}
\[
\begin{aligned}
V &= \frac{25}{840}\ \mathrm{m^3} \approx 0.0297619\ \mathrm{m^3},\\[4pt]
s &= \left(\frac{0.0297619}{\pi}\right)^{1/3} \approx 0.2116,\\[4pt]
\frac{S}{S_0} &= s^2 \approx 0.0447721 \quad(\text{PuffSat area } \approx 4.48\%\ \text{of GOCE}),\\[4pt]
\text{Equivalently: } & S_0 \approx 22.335 \times S.
\end{aligned}
\]

\noindent\textbf{Remark:} geometric scaling predicts \(\sim22\times\) reduction in area.  To remain conservative we \emph{assume} only a \(\times 10\) reduction in drag. Explicitly: although area scales \(\approx22\times\) smaller, we take drag to be only \(10\times\) smaller.

\subsection*{Baseline thrust assumption}
GOCE used up to \(\SI{20}{\milli\newton}\) for drag free control (solar minimum).  We  allow up to \(\times 10\) larger thrust during solar maximum. This gives \(\SI{200}{\milli\newton}\) as a worst-case GOCE-equivalent value.  This solar maximum penalty is probably conservative, since we can time PuffSats to enter low atmosphere at night.  Applying the pessimistic \(\times 10\) weaker drag for the PuffSat (above) yields a continuous required thrust of
\[
F_1 = \frac{\SI{200}{\milli\newton}}{10} = \SI{20}{\milli\newton}
\]
at the representative altitude (\(\sim\)\SI{250}{\kilo\metre}) used in this estimate. 

We further impose a short-duration surge case: an instantaneous increase in drag by a factor of \(20\) for \(t_2=\SI{30}{\s}\) between \(\SI{250}{\kilo\metre}\) and \(\SI{200}{\kilo\metre}\). This deliberately conservative bound requires
\[
F_2 = 20\times F_1 = \SI{400}{\milli\newton}
\]
for \(t_2=\SI{30}{\s}\).

\subsection*{Cold-gas mass flow}
For a thruster with specific impulse \(I_{\mathrm{sp}}\) (seconds) and \(g_0=\SI{9.80665}{\m\per\s^2}\),
\[
\dot m = \frac{F}{I_{\mathrm{sp}} g_0}.
\]
We use a pessimistic \(I_{\mathrm{sp}}=\SI{25}{\s}\), about half a typical CubeSat cold-gas value \cite{tummala2017cubesat} to account for miniaturization losses. This gives
\[
\dot m_{\text{per }1\ \mathrm{mN}} = \frac{1\times 10^{-3}}{25\cdot 9.80665}
\approx 4.079\times 10^{-6}\ \mathrm{kg/s} \approx 4.079\ \mathrm{mg/s}.
\]

\subsection*{Fuel required for the two intervals}
\paragraph{Continuous interval.}
Required thrust \(F_1=\SI{20}{\milli\newton}\) sustained for \(t_1=\SI{300}{\s}\):
\[
m_1 = \frac{F_1 t_1}{I_{\mathrm{sp}} g_0}
= \frac{(20\times 10^{-3})(300)}{25\cdot 9.80665}
\approx \SI{24.473}{\g}.
\]

\paragraph{Short surge interval.}
Surge thrust \(F_2=\SI{400}{\milli\newton}\) sustained for \(t_2=\SI{30}{\s}\):
\[
m_2 = \frac{F_2 t_2}{I_{\mathrm{sp}} g_0}
= \frac{(400\times 10^{-3})(30)}{25\cdot 9.80665}
\approx \SI{48.946}{\g}.
\]

\paragraph{Baseline propellant:}
\[
m_{\mathrm{base}} = m_1 + m_2 \approx \SI{73.4196}{\g}.
\]

\subsection*{Margins and final rounding}
Apply the following conservative margins.  GOCE operated at a representative altitude of $\approx \SI{250}{\kilo\meter}$ and successfully descended to as low as $\SI{229}{\kilo\meter}$ \cite{goce_229km}. Atmospheric density rises sharply at lower altitudes. We model an instantaneous drag jump as PuffSat descends from $\SI{250}{\kilo\meter}$ to our \SI{200}{\kilo\meter} target. GOCE's designers treated $\SI{250}{\kilo\meter}$ as nominal because it offered more reliable operational conditions.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Add \(\SI{20}{\g}\) for attitude/torque control: \(m_{\mathrm{a}} = m_{\mathrm{base}} + \SI{20}{\g} \approx \SI{93.4196}{\g}.\)
  \item Double for pulsing inefficiencies and other control limitations: \(m_{\mathrm{b}} = 2 m_{\mathrm{a}} \approx \SI{186.8392}{\g}.\)
  \item Apply an additional doubling factor to account for drag at PuffSat's higher velocity, which scales as \(v^2\) (since $\frac{(\text{PuffSat's speed})^2}{(\text{GOCE's speed})^2} = \frac{\left(\SI{11}{\kilo\meter\per\second}\right)^2}{\left(\SI{8}{\kilo\meter\per\second}\right)^2}
 \approx 2$): \(m_{\mathrm{c}} = 2 m_{\mathrm{b}} \approx \SI{373.6784}{\g}.\)

\end{enumerate}

Round up for presentation/engineering margin:
\[
\boxed{m_{\mathrm{req}} \approx \SI{374}{\g}\ \text{(computed value); rounded to }\SI{400}{\g}\ \text{for conservative margin}.}
\]

\subsection*{Notes and explicit assumptions}
\begin{itemize}
  \item We explicitly made two large conservative choices: (1) although geometric area scaling predicts \(\sim22\times\) lower drag, we assume only a \(10\times\) reduction (pessimistic), and (2) we include an extreme short-duration surge of \(20\times\) the baseline drag for \(\SI{30}{\s}\).
  \item The specific impulse \(I_{\mathrm{sp}}=\SI{25}{\s}\) is pessimistic. A higher \(I_{\mathrm{sp}}\) reduces propellant need proportionally.
  \item The final \(\SI{400}{\g}\) figure is a rounded engineering margin. The computed baseline with margins is \(\approx\SI{374}{\g}\).
  \item Surface area calculations did not include the nitrogen tank proposed in \autoref{sec:formation_challenges_current_missions}.  We considered a gas generator alternative and assumed it would be compact, changing the PuffSat's shape negligibly.   If a nitrogen tank was used, drag would increase since the tank is a reasonable fraction of the PuffSat's size.
  \item This is a conservative back-of-the-napkin upper bound. Second-order effects are not modeled.
  \item A preliminary closed-loop control simulation confirms this bound from the other direction. Where this appendix estimates propellant from GOCE drag scaling alone, the simulation flies the full interception: corrector burns, terminal guidance, finite-thrust execution, and a dispersed environment. It still holds the total guidance propellant under 2\% of the \SI{25}{\kilo\gram} PuffSat mass, even in the worst sampled case \cite{Katz_aim_is_all_you_need_2025}.
  \item \textbf{Acknowledgement:} ChatGPT extensively helped with this derivation and much of the text is copied from it verbatim \cite{chatgpt}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Derivation Of PuffSat Mass/Rocket Mass Continuous Approximation}\label{sec:PuffSat_ratio_approximation}  We want to approximate the ratio of total PuffSat mass \(m_p\) to rocket mass \(m_r\). A sequence of PuffSats, all traveling at velocity \(v_p\), pushes the rocket from initial velocity \(v_{ri}\) to final velocity \(v_{rf}\).   First, assume every collision is perfectly elastic in one dimension.   We model the rocket as continuously bombarded by infinitesimal PuffSats, each with mass \(dm_p\), and solve for a closed-form expression.   (Note: Grok \cite{grok}  helped with some of the math for this derivation and parts of this derivation are copied from Grok  results directly)

The initial velocities are
\begin{itemize}
\item Mass \( m_r \): Velocity \( v_r \).
\item PuffSat: Mass \( dm_p \), velocity \( v_p \).
\end{itemize}
After the collision, let:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Mass \( m_r \): Velocity \( v_r + dv_r \).
    \item PuffSat: Velocity \( v_p' \).
\end{itemize}
\subsection{By Conservation Of Momentum} \[
m_r v_r + dm_p v_p = m_r (v_r + dv_r) + dm_p v_p'
\]
\[
m_r v_r + dm_p v_p = m_r v_r + m_r dv_r + dm_p v_p'
\]
\[
dm_p v_p = m_r dv_r + dm_p v_p'
\]
\begin{equation}
m_r dv_r = dm_p (v_p - v_p') \label{eq:momentum}
\end{equation}

\subsection[Velocity Change Of Rocket Mass]{Velocity Change Of \(m_r\)}
For an elastic collision
\begin{equation}
    v_p'\ = \frac{2m_r}{m_r+m_p}v_r + \frac{m_p-m_r}{m_r+m_p}v_p \label{eq:vb_prime_full_momentum}
\end{equation}
Since \(m_r \gg m_p\)   we plug \(m_p = 0\)  into \autoref{eq:vb_prime_full_momentum} and find 
\begin{equation}
v_p' = 2v_r - v_p  \label{eq:vb_prime_result}
\end{equation}      

Substituting \(v_p'\) from  \autoref{eq:vb_prime_result} into \autoref{eq:momentum}:
\[
m_r dv_r = dm_p (v_p - (2 v_r - v_p))
\]
\[
m_r dv_r = dm_p (v_p - 2 v_r + v_p)
\]
\[
m_r dv_r = dm_p (2 v_p - 2 v_r)
\]
\[
m_r dv_r = 2 dm_p (v_p - v_r)
\]
\begin{equation}
dv_r = \frac{2 (v_p - v_r)}{m_r} dm_p \label{eq:vchange_mr}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Integrate Over Many Collisions}
The velocity of \( m_r \) changes from \( v_{ri} \) to \( v_{rf} \) as more PuffSats collide. Integrate \autoref{eq:vchange_mr}:
\[
\int_{v_{ri}}^{v_{rf}} dv_r = \int_0^{m_p} \frac{2 (v_p - v_r)}{m_r} dm_p
\]
The left-hand side is:
\[
\int_{v_{ri}}^{v_{rf}} dv_r = v_{rf} - v_{ri}
\]
For the right-hand side, treat \( v_r \) as a function of the accumulated PuffSat mass \( m_p \). From \autoref{eq:vchange_mr}:
\begin{equation}
\frac{dv_r}{v_p - v_r} = \frac{2 dm_p}{m_r}\label{eq:dvr_velocity_relation}
\end{equation}

Since \(v_p\) is constant,  \(dv_p = 0\) and  \(dv_r = -d(v_p-v_r)\), so we can rewrite \autoref{eq:dvr_velocity_relation} as:
\[
-\frac{d(v_p - v_r)}{v_p - v_r} = \frac{2 dm_p}{m_r}
\]
Integrate both sides:
- Left: \( \int_{v_p - v_{ri}}^{v_p - v_{rf}} -\frac{d(v_p - v_r)}{v_p - v_r} = \int_{v_{ri}}^{v_{rf}} \frac{dv_r}{v_p - v_r} \)
\[
= \left[ -\ln|v_p - v_r| \right]_{v_{ri}}^{v_{rf}} = \ln \left| \frac{v_p - v_{ri}}{v_p - v_{rf}} \right|
\]
- Right: \( \int_0^{m_p} \frac{2 dm_p}{m_r} = \frac{2 m_p}{m_r} \)
Equate:
\[
\ln \left| \frac{v_p - v_{ri}}{v_p - v_{rf}} \right| = \frac{2 m_p}{m_r}
\]
Solve for \( m_p \):
\[
\left| \frac{v_p - v_{ri}}{v_p - v_{rf}} \right| = e^{2 m_p / m_r}
\]
\begin{equation}
m_p = \frac{m_r}{2} \ln \left| \frac{v_p - v_{ri}}{v_p - v_{rf}} \right| 
\end{equation}
\subsection{Interpret The Absolute Value}   
The absolute value accounts for the direction of velocities:
- If \( v_p > v_{ri} \) and \( v_p > v_{rf} \),  then \( v_p - v_{ri} \) and \( v_p - v_{rf} \) are positive, so:
\[
m_p = \frac{m_r}{2} \ln \left( \frac{v_p - v_{ri}}{v_p - v_{rf}} \right)
\]
\subsection{Compute PuffSat To Rocket Mass Ratio, With A Fudge Factor For Real World Losses}

Solve for the PuffSat-to-rocket mass ratio. Add a fudge factor $f$ in $(0,1)$ for restitution losses and imperfect volatile spread off the pusher plate.   Assume $f$ is constant across collisions. In practice it varies with the relative PuffSat-rocket velocity.  PuffSat sizes will in practice grow as the rocket nears $v_p$, since more mass is needed for the same acceleration.
\begin{equation}
\frac{m_r}{m_p} = \frac{2f}{\ln\left(\frac{v_p-v_{ri}}{v_p-v_{rf}}\right)}\label{eq:PuffSat_ratio}
\end{equation}

\section{Derivation Of Optimal Cable Taper}\label{sec:breaking_length_derived}

\paragraph{AI Acknowledgment}
Most of this derivation is copied directly from a solution worked out with a ChatGPT prompt \cite{chatgpt}.

\paragraph{Assumptions and notation.}  
Let $x$ denote the vertical coordinate measured downward from the top support ($x=0$ at the top, $x=l$ at the bottom).  
The material has density $\rho$ (mass per unit volume), subject to gravitational acceleration $g$.  

The ultimate tensile strength (stress limit) of the material is $\sigma$.  
Define the \emph{breaking length}
\[
b \;\equiv\; \frac{\sigma}{\rho g},
\]
i.e.\ a uniform vertical cable of length $b$ will break under its own weight.  

Let $A(x)$ be the cross-sectional area at height $x$, and $T(x)$ the internal tension at that section.  
A payload $P$ may be attached at the bottom ($x=l$). If no external load is present, set $P=0$.  

\paragraph{Equilibrium condition.}  
Static force balance gives
\[
T(x) \;=\; P + \rho g \int_x^l A(s)\,ds,
\]
where the integral accounts for the weight of the cable below $x$.  

\paragraph{Optimality condition.}  
To minimize mass for given $P$ and $l$, the stress constraint is active everywhere:
\[
T(x) \;=\; \sigma A(x).
\]

Differentiating with respect to $x$,
\[
\sigma A'(x) \;=\; T'(x).
\]
But
\[
T'(x) \;=\; -\,\rho g\, A(x),
\]
so that
\[
A'(x) \;=\; -\frac{\rho g}{\sigma}\, A(x) \;=\; -\frac{1}{b}\,A(x).
\]

\paragraph{Solution.}  
This linear ODE has the exponential solution
\[
A(x) \;=\; C\, e^{-x/b},
\]
for some constant $C$ determined by the boundary condition (e.g.\ the required cross-sectional area at the top or bottom).

\section{Deriving Effective Exhaust Velocity for Idealized 100\% Efficient Prograde/Retrograde PuffSat Collision Rocket Thrust}\label{sec:dv_effective}
Suppose a retrograde PuffSat with mass $m_{rp}$ and a prograde PuffSat with mass $m_{pp}$ collide in a pulsed rocket propulsion chamber, as depicted in \autoref{tab:pulsed_combustion_illustration}.  We want the maximum exhaust velocity $v_e$ for the prograde rocket, assuming 100\% engine efficiency. All the combined gas is expelled behind us, in the retrograde direction.   For simplicity we say 
\begin{equation}
m_{rp} + m_{pp} = 1\label{eq:mass_is_1}
\end{equation} 
Each PuffSat has velocity $v_p$ so the retrograde PuffSat has velocity $2v_p$ in the reference frame of the prograde PuffSat.   In the prograde reference frame, kinetic energy is
\begin{equation}
E = \frac{m_{rp} (2v_p)^2}{2} = 2m_{rp}v_p^2\label{eq:ke_PuffSats}
\end{equation}

Let $v_g$ be gas exhaust velocity expelled from the rocket, and assume the expelled gas mass is infinitesimal compared to the entire prograde rocket and its payload.  Applying mass from \autoref{eq:mass_is_1}
 and kinetic energy from \autoref{eq:ke_PuffSats}, we have 
 \begin{equation}
 2m_{rp}v_p^2= \frac{v_g^2}{2}
 \end{equation}
 and solving for $v_g$ we get 
 \begin{equation}
 v_g = \sqrt{4v_p^2m_{rp}} = 2v_p\sqrt{m_{rp}} \label{eq:vg_result}
 \end{equation}
 Total momentum change is 
 \[(m_{rp} + m_{pp})v_g - 2v_pm_{rp} = v_g-2v_pm_{rp} = 2v_p(\sqrt{m_{rp}} - m_{rp}) \]
Differentiating and setting equal to zero gives
 \begin{equation}
 m_{rp} = \frac{1}{4}, \label{eq:max_m_rp}
 v_g = v_p,
 v_e= \frac{v_p}{2}
 \end{equation}

\section{Flight-Path Angle And Downward Pulse Fraction At Interception}\label{sec:down_angle_geometry}
This appendix backs two figures from \autoref{sec:lightweight_pusher_plates}: the \SI{8.5}{\degree} flight-path angle at interception, and the growth of each pulse's downward fraction from about 15\% to roughly one-half as the craft accelerates.

A PuffSat rides a Keplerian ellipse with perigee radius $r_p = R_\oplus + \SI{50}{\kilo\meter}$, the disposal perigee of \autoref{sec:handling_space_debris}, and a near-escape apogee, taken here as $r_a = R_\oplus + \SI{150000}{\kilo\meter}$ with $R_\oplus = \SI{6378}{\kilo\meter}$. Its semi-major axis, eccentricity, and semi-latus rectum are
\begin{equation}
a = \frac{r_p + r_a}{2}, \qquad e = \frac{r_a - r_p}{r_a + r_p}, \qquad p = a\,(1 - e^2).
\end{equation}
At the interception radius $r = R_\oplus + \SI{200}{\kilo\meter}$ the speed follows from the vis-viva equation, and the specific angular momentum $h = \sqrt{\mu p}$ is conserved, so the flight-path angle $\gamma$ below the local horizontal obeys the standard two-body relations \cite{vallado_astrodynamics}
\begin{equation}
v = \sqrt{\mu\!\left(\frac{2}{r} - \frac{1}{a}\right)}, \qquad \cos\gamma = \frac{h}{r\,v} = \frac{\sqrt{\mu p}}{r\,v},
\end{equation}
where $\mu = \SI{3.986e5}{\kilo\meter\cubed\per\second\squared}$ is the Earth's gravitational parameter. These give $v \approx \SI{10.8}{\kilo\meter\per\second}$ and $\gamma \approx \SI{8.5}{\degree}$, so $\sin\gamma \approx 0.15$. The low perigee sets the angle, not the apogee. Sweeping the apogee from \SI{30000}{\kilo\meter} to \SI{300000}{\kilo\meter} moves $\gamma$ only across \SIrange{7.9}{8.6}{\degree}.

The same interception velocity resolves into a forward component $v\cos\gamma \approx \SI{10.7}{\kilo\meter\per\second}$ and a downward component $v\sin\gamma \approx \SI{1.6}{\kilo\meter\per\second}$. The craft climbs prograde, and near orbital insertion its velocity is nearly horizontal with speed $v_c$, which builds from rest toward the circular value $v_{\mathrm{circ}} = \sqrt{\mu/r} \approx \SI{7.8}{\kilo\meter\per\second}$. Each pulse delivers momentum along the relative velocity $\vec{u} = \vec{v} - \vec{v}_c$. Its forward part is the overtaking velocity $v\cos\gamma - v_c$, which shrinks as the craft speeds up, while its downward part holds fixed at $v\sin\gamma$. The downward fraction of the pulse is then
\begin{equation}
\frac{v\sin\gamma}{\sqrt{(v\sin\gamma)^2 + (v\cos\gamma - v_c)^2}}.
\label{eq:down_fraction}
\end{equation}
At $v_c = 0$ the relative velocity is the PuffSat's own velocity, and the fraction equals $\sin\gamma \approx 15\%$. That is the floor. As $v_c$ approaches $v_{\mathrm{circ}}$ the overtaking velocity collapses to about \SI{2.9}{\kilo\meter\per\second} while the downward \SI{1.6}{\kilo\meter\per\second} holds, and \autoref{eq:down_fraction} rises to roughly one-half, near 49\%. This worst case sits late in the boost, where the craft is fastest and the residual down-force after the plate's reflection is largest.

\section{Tensile-Fiber Selection for PuffSat Tension Members}\label{sec:fiber_selection}
Three PuffSat structures carry organic fiber under steady tension above most of the atmosphere: the vacuum launch column (\autoref{sec:tether_tapering}), the Mars skyhook tether (\autoref{sec:mars_skyhook}), and the polymer net behind the pusher-plate sail (\autoref{sec:lightweight_pusher_plates}). The same four fibers are candidates in each case, but a different property decides the choice. The column turns on self-support length, the skyhook on creep and specific strength, and the sail net on shock toughness within a temperature-ceiling limit. \autoref{tab:fiber_selection} collects the numbers, and this section gives the reasoning the three sites share.

Self-support breaking length scales with specific strength. PBO, the fiber sold as Zylon, reaches a self-support length near \SI{380}{\kilo\meter}, about \num{1.6} times Vectran's \cite{zylon_technical_info}. Kevlar and SK99 Dyneema fall short of the \SIrange{300}{400}{\kilo\meter} a tall column spans \cite{cambridge_materials_databook, dyneema_sk99_datasheet}, and Vectran is shorter still. Taper closes that gap, but its mass penalty climbs steeply as self-support length drops, so the column favors the fiber with the most margin (see \autoref{sec:tether_tapering}). Creep separates the fibers under permanent load. Drawn polyethylene creeps even when carbon-modified for creep resistance \cite{sk99_tension}. Vectran, a liquid-crystal polymer, all but eliminates creep \cite{vectran_brochure}, and PBO creeps about as little as Vectran \cite{zylon_technical_info}. A structure that holds steady tension for years, such as a spinning skyhook, rules out polyethylene on creep alone.

Temperature ceiling forms a ladder. Drawn polyethylene like Dyneema melts near \SI{147}{\celsius} \cite{uhmwpe_fiber_datasheet}. Vectran holds strength to roughly \SI{200}{\celsius} and melts above \SI{300}{\celsius} \cite{vectran_brochure}. Aramid does not melt, keeps most of its strength to about \SI{250}{\celsius}, and decomposes above \SI{425}{\celsius} \cite{kevlar_technical_guide}. PBO tops the ladder and decomposes only near \SI{650}{\celsius} \cite{zylon_technical_info}. A back ply that runs hot must climb the ladder, while one that radiates cool between widely spaced pulses can sit lower.

Cold sits at the safe end of that ladder. A drawn or rigid-rod fiber loses strength as it warms, not as it cools. PBO holds its strength down to cryogenic temperature. A Zylon/epoxy composite tested at \SI{77}{\kelvin} keeps a fiber tensile strength above \SI{4.3}{\giga\pascal}, which is why it reinforces high-field magnet windings \cite{zylon_cryogenic}. A night-side pass on an Earth column or a Mars tether stays far warmer than that, so the cold does not weaken the load-bearing fiber. The property to watch is brittleness, and it is not a cold effect. PBO is a low-strain fiber at every temperature. Its elongation at break is \SIrange{2.5}{3.5}{\percent}, and it fails by axial splitting with little plastic flow \cite{zylon_technical_info}. It has no glass transition in this range, so it shows no cold ductile-to-brittle transition like the one that embrittles ordinary plastics. The transition it does show is set by strain rate, not temperature. A fast pull or a shock load fractures it with little warning \cite{pbo_strain_rate_dbt}.

A static tension member tolerates a brittle fiber. The launch column and the skyhook tether hold a steady axial load, where strength and low creep decide and elongation does not. Brittleness bites only where the fiber folds, knots, bends over a small radius, or takes a shock. The architecture keeps PBO clear of those cases. Terminations spread load by potting or capstan wraps rather than knots. Bend radii stay generous. The curvature actuators of \autoref{sec:vacuum_tube_details} ramp slowly, so the line never sees an impulsive spike. Redundant parallel cables cover a fiber that nicks rather than yields, since it cannot shed a defect by flowing around it. Parts that must fold or flex, such as the corrugated lower tube wall or the pusher-plate sail net, use Vectran instead. Vectran resists flex-cracking when cold, the property that won it over aramid for the Mars Pathfinder airbags, where folded aramid cracked along its creases \cite{pathfinder_airbag}.

The sail net pushes that logic furthest, because it is the one tension member loaded by shock rather than steady weight. Each pulse arrives as a brief, high strain-rate impulse, the regime where PBO turns brittle \cite{pbo_strain_rate_dbt}. The net exists to absorb that impulse, so shock toughness decides the choice and PBO's specific-strength lead does not apply. Vectran takes the role. It holds the impulse without creep, so the sail stays taut across a long pulse train \cite{vectran_brochure}. Temperature is the one limit on that choice. Vectran keeps useful strength only to about \SI{200}{\celsius}, far below PBO's \SI{650}{\celsius} \cite{vectran_brochure, zylon_technical_info}. The stitched laminate exists to keep the back ply under that ceiling. The ceramic felt insulates it, and a brief, widely spaced pulse radiates away before its heat reaches the net (see \autoref{sec:lightweight_pusher_plates}). While the back-ply equilibrium temperature stays below \SI{200}{\celsius}, Vectran wins outright. A hotter or more tightly spaced pulse train, or a thinner felt budget, forces the net up the ladder to aramid or coated PBO for thermal survival. That climb trades shock toughness for thermal headroom, paid back with a thicker net or wider pulse spacing.

Three environmental weaknesses shape whichever fiber gets exposed. Atomic oxygen erodes organic polymers in low orbit \cite{ao_protection_banks}. Ultraviolet weakens aramid, Vectran, and PBO. Moisture drives slow hydrolysis in aramid \cite{kevlar_technical_guide}, and PBO shares that weakness. PBO is the most exposed of the four. Bare PBO sheds about a third of its strength in a few hours of atomic oxygen \cite{pbo_atomic_oxygen}, and ultraviolet or even visible light breaks its oxazole ring and can cost it half its strength within months, the failure that ended its use in police body armor \cite{zylon_body_armor}. One continuous silica or metallized barrier answers these weaknesses together. It reflects the ultraviolet and seals out the atomic oxygen at once, the same low-outgassing inorganic coating that has kept polyimide solar-array blankets intact in low orbit for years \cite{ao_protection_banks}, and behind it the fiber stays dry so the hydrolysis pathway shuts. A barrier helps only while it stays continuous. Micrometeoroid strikes and thermal cycling can open pinholes or hairline cracks, and atomic oxygen then undercuts the fiber at each defect, so the coating carries a thin borosilicate or silicone overlay that reflows over a fresh scratch before the fiber loses much strength \cite{self_healing_material_survey}. Once that barrier holds, PBO's specific strength and temperature ceiling come at the price of keeping it intact, and the self-healing overlay is the least-proven part of that scheme. That is why PBO is the default where the environment is controlled and a liability where it is not, such as humid air near the surface.

PBO is not new to spaceflight. Metal-clad PBO is a stock product, and the fiber already carries the suspension loads of Crew Dragon's main parachutes as a space-rated tension member \cite{crew_dragon_parachute, zylon_technical_info}.
